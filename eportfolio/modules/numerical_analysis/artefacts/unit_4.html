Unit 4: Probabilities, Probability Distributions, and Sampling

← Back to artefacts
Activity 1

Basic Probability — Marble Sampling

This activity used an interactive marble-sampling demonstration to explore the foundations of simple probability. By adjusting the number of red, blue, and yellow marbles in a virtual sample space, I could observe directly how the probability of drawing any given colour is determined by the composition of the whole collection.

P(colour) = Number of marbles of that colour ÷ Total marbles

Learning Outcomes

1

Basic Probability Concepts

Learned to calculate simple probabilities from counts within a sample space, and understood how the formula P = favourable / total applies directly to concrete examples.

2

Estimation Before Verification

Practised formulating probability estimates intuitively before checking answers, strengthening the ability to reason about likelihoods without relying solely on formulas.

3

Dynamic Input and Output

Observed how increasing or decreasing marbles of a given colour adjusts all probabilities simultaneously, reinforcing that probabilities depend entirely on the composition of the sample space.

4

Proportion and Ratio Thinking

Developed a stronger sense of how proportions relate to probability. The interactive format made it easier to treat probability as a ratio rather than an abstract formula.

5

Identifying Intuitive Biases

By comparing estimates with correct answers, identified patterns where intuition tended to underestimate or overestimate probabilities — a direct step towards more calibrated probabilistic thinking.

6

Theory to Practice

Using a dynamic visual tool bridged the gap between formula-based learning and practical understanding, applying theoretical probability concepts in a hands-on context.

Activity 1 made probability feel tangible rather than abstract. The most valuable moment was comparing my pre-calculation estimates with the actual values — it revealed specific situations where I systematically over- or underestimated likelihood, which formulaic study alone would not have surfaced. The interactive nature of the tool also reinforced that probability is not a fixed property of an outcome but a ratio that shifts every time the composition of the sample space changes.
Activity 3

Discrete and Continuous Probability Distributions

This activity explored seven probability distributions — four discrete and three continuous — through interactive simulations. For each distribution, the focus was on understanding the governing parameters, how changing them shapes the distribution, and what real-world processes each distribution models.

Discrete Probability Distributions

Binomial Distribution

Parameters: n (trials), p (success probability)

  • Models the number of successes in a fixed number of independent trials.
  • Changing n and p directly shifts the shape, centre, and spread of the distribution.
  • Applications include quality control, clinical trial outcomes, and binary event modelling.

Poisson Distribution

Parameter: λ (average rate)

  • Represents the probability of a given number of events occurring in a fixed interval at a constant average rate.
  • Increasing λ shifts the distribution rightward and broadens its spread.
  • Applications include arrival modelling, call-centre traffic, and rare event analysis.

Hypergeometric Distribution

Parameters: N (population), K (successes in population), n (sample size)

  • Differs from the binomial by involving sampling without replacement, making trials dependent.
  • Population size, number of successes, and sample size all interact to determine probabilities.
  • Applications include card draws, quality inspection, and finite population sampling.
Continuous Probability Distributions

Normal Distribution

Parameters: μ (mean), σ (standard deviation)

  • Models continuous data that clusters symmetrically around a mean.
  • μ controls the centre; σ controls the width and flatness of the bell curve.
  • Fundamental in inferential statistics due to the Central Limit Theorem.

Uniform Distribution

Parameters: a (lower bound), b (upper bound)

  • Assigns equal probability to all values in an interval [a, b].
  • The range limits determine the width and height of the constant density function.
  • Examples include random number generators and equal-chance selection systems.

Exponential Distribution

Parameter: λ (rate)

  • Models waiting times between independent events at a constant rate.
  • The rate parameter controls the steepness of the exponential decay.
  • Applications include time-to-failure analysis, queueing systems, and reliability modelling.

Gaussian Distribution

Parameters: μ, σ (discrete and continuous forms)

  • Essentially the continuous normal distribution; explored here in both continuous and discrete-approximation forms.
  • Emerges naturally in processes involving the aggregation of many independent random variables.
  • Ubiquitous in natural and statistical processes — from measurement error to financial returns.
Working through seven distributions in a single activity was demanding but rewarding. The most clarifying contrast was between the binomial and hypergeometric distributions: both count successes across a number of draws, but independence (sampling with replacement) versus dependence (sampling without replacement) produces meaningfully different probability structures — a distinction that is easy to overlook when learning from formulas alone. Similarly, seeing the Poisson distribution shift and widen as λ increased made the abstract rate parameter feel concrete. On the continuous side, the exponential distribution's connection to the Poisson — both governed by a constant rate, one counting events and one measuring gaps between them — was a particularly useful link to recognise. Overall, this activity deepened my understanding of how the choice of distribution is not arbitrary but reflects specific assumptions about the data-generating process.

Reflection

Unit 4 marked a shift from data manipulation in R to the mathematical foundations that underpin statistical reasoning. Activity 1 established the intuitive groundwork — probability as a ratio of favourable to total outcomes — while Activity 3 extended that foundation across a range of named distributions, each encoding different assumptions about how randomness operates in the real world.

The most important conceptual gain across the unit was learning to think in terms of parameters: every distribution is not a single fixed object but a family of shapes controlled by one or two numbers. Adjusting μ and σ, or n and p, or λ, produces a different member of the same family — and choosing the right parameter values is what links the abstract distribution to a specific real-world scenario.

This unit also reinforced the Central Limit Theorem as a unifying idea: regardless of the underlying distribution, the sampling distribution of the mean converges to a normal distribution as sample size grows. That result connects Activity 3's discrete and continuous distributions back to the normal distribution at the centre, and explains why the normal distribution occupies such a privileged position in statistical practice.

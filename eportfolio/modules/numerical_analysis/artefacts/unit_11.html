Data Activity 9

Building directly on the correlation analysis from Unit 10, this activity fitted a simple linear regression model to predict diastolic blood pressure (DBP) from systolic blood pressure (SBP) using the Health_Data.sav dataset. The aims were to derive the population regression equation, assess statistical significance at the 5% level, evaluate model fit, check residual assumptions, and demonstrate practical prediction.

Fitting the Regression Model

Estimated Population Regression Equation DBP̂ = 19.41 + 0.496 × SBP

The equation states that for every 1 mmHg increase in systolic blood pressure, diastolic blood pressure is predicted to increase by approximately 0.5 mmHg on average. The intercept (19.41) represents the estimated DBP when SBP equals zero — a value that lies outside the meaningful physiological range and should not be interpreted clinically.

SBP is a statistically significant predictor of DBP at the 5% level (t = 22.96, p < 0.001). The model accounts for 71.7% of the variance in diastolic blood pressure (R² = 0.717), indicating a strong and well-fitting model.

R² = 0.717 means that systolic blood pressure alone explains approximately 71.7% of the variability in diastolic blood pressure across individuals in this dataset. The remaining 28.3% is attributable to other factors not included in this single-predictor model — such as age, weight, medication, or lifestyle variables. The F-statistic (527.2, p < 0.001) confirms that the overall model is significantly better than predicting DBP from the mean alone.

Regression Line on Scatter Plot


The scatter plot with the overlaid regression line shows that the linear model fits the data well — points cluster closely around the line with consistent spread, and there are no obvious non-linear patterns or influential outlier clusters. Using lwd = 2 renders the trend line thicker and more legible against the point cloud. The abline(model) call draws the line directly from the fitted model object, ensuring it is exactly aligned with the estimated equation.

Diagnostic Checks

Normality of Residuals

Shapiro–Wilk: W = 0.993, p = 0.471

p > 0.05 — no evidence of departure from normality. Residuals are approximately normally distributed, satisfying a key assumption of OLS regression.

Diagnostic Plots

par(mfrow = c(2, 2)); plot(model)

The four-panel plot checks residuals vs fitted values (linearity and homoscedasticity), Q-Q plot of residuals (normality), scale-location (constant variance), and Cook's distance (influential observations).

✓

All checked assumptions are satisfied. The Shapiro–Wilk test on residuals is non-significant (p = 0.471), confirming normality. The OLS regression results are therefore valid and can be interpreted with confidence.

Practical Prediction

SBP (input)Predicted DBP95% CI Lower95% CI Upper
140 mmHg 88.85 mmHg 87.85 mmHg 89.85 mmHg
For an individual with a systolic blood pressure of 140 mmHg, the model predicts a diastolic blood pressure of approximately 88.85 mmHg. The 95% confidence interval (87.85 – 89.85 mmHg) is narrow — a range of just 2 mmHg — reflecting the precision of this estimate. This narrowness is consistent with the high R² of the model: when a predictor explains 71.7% of the variance, predictions made within the observed range of SBP values will be highly precise. Note that this is a confidence interval for the mean response at SBP = 140, not a prediction interval for an individual — the latter would be considerably wider.
Confidence interval vs prediction interval: interval = "confidence" estimates the plausible range for the average DBP among all individuals with SBP = 140 mmHg. To estimate the range within which a single new individual's DBP would fall, use interval = "prediction" instead — this produces a wider interval because it accounts for individual-level variability in addition to uncertainty in the mean estimate.

What did I learn?

This task showed how regression extends correlation: while Unit 10 established that SBP and DBP are strongly associated (ρ = 0.80), regression quantified how much DBP changes per unit increase in SBP (0.496 mmHg) and enabled actual predictions with confidence intervals. I also learned that R² and the F-statistic serve different purposes — R² describes how much variance is explained, while the F-test asks whether the model is significantly better than a null model of no predictors.

Why check residual normality if SBP itself was non-normal?

OLS regression requires that the residuals — not the predictors — are approximately normally distributed. A non-normal predictor does not automatically produce non-normal residuals. The Shapiro–Wilk test on residuals(model) (W = 0.993, p = 0.471) confirmed that this assumption was satisfied, making the inference valid despite SBP's non-normality.

How will this help in future work?

Simple linear regression is the foundation for multiple regression, logistic regression, and many machine learning models. Understanding how to fit, interpret, validate, and predict from a regression model — including the distinction between confidence and prediction intervals — provides a rigorous base for more complex modelling tasks in data science and AI.

Overall Reflection

Unit 11 completed the analytical arc that began in Unit 10. Correlation established the existence and strength of the SBP–DBP relationship; regression quantified it precisely, assigned a formal equation, and made the relationship practically usable for prediction. This progression — from association to model to inference — mirrors the workflow of real applied analysis.

The diagnostic step was the most technically instructive part of the activity. The four-panel plot(model) output and the Shapiro–Wilk test on residuals turned the model from a black box into a verified object. Without assumption checking, a model's standard errors, p-values, and confidence intervals cannot be trusted — a principle that applies equally to logistic regression, mixed models, and many machine learning algorithms that share OLS-like assumptions.

The prediction exercise also introduced an important distinction that is often glossed over: the difference between estimating the mean response at a given input (confidence interval) versus predicting an individual outcome (prediction interval). Communicating this distinction clearly is part of responsible quantitative reporting, particularly in health contexts where precise language around uncertainty directly affects how results are understood and acted upon.

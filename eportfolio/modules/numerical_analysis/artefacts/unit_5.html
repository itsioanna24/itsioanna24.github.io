Data Activity 4

This activity focused on data visualisation in R, using the COVID-19 India dataset to produce four distinct chart types: a bar chart, a pie chart, a histogram, and a line chart. Each question targeted a different aspect of the data and a different visualisation technique, building a practical toolkit for exploratory data analysis.

Question 1

Bar Chart of COVID-19 Reports by Region

▮ Bar Chart — barplot()

The first task was to summarise how many COVID-19 observations were recorded for each Indian State and Union Territory, and to display those frequencies as a bar chart.

  • table() was used to count how many COVID-19 observations were recorded for each State / Union Territory.
  • The resulting frequency table shows which states had the highest number of reported entries and which had fewer.
  • A bar chart produced with barplot() visually compared these frequencies across all regions.
  • The chart clearly highlights the states with the largest volume of records, which may correspond to higher reporting rates, larger populations, or earlier outbreak timing.

What was the task?

To produce a bar chart illustrating how many COVID-19 reports were recorded for each Indian State and Union Territory, using table() to compute frequencies and barplot() to visualise them.

What did I do?

I imported the dataset, confirmed column names with names(), applied table() to the state variable, and rendered the result as a bar chart with rotated axis labels to improve readability.

What were the results?

Some states had significantly more reported entries than others. The bar chart made these differences immediately visible, showing which regions dominated the dataset.

What did I learn?

I learned how to summarise categorical data with table() and visualise it using barplot(). I also saw how label parameters like las and cex.names are essential for legibility when there are many categories.

How will this help in future work?

Quickly summarising and visualising categorical distributions is a fundamental step in any exploratory analysis. These skills will carry directly into future assignments and professional reporting where communicating data patterns clearly is essential.

Question 2

Pie Chart of Case Severity Levels by Nationality

■ Pie Chart — pie()

The second task required aggregating confirmed case counts by nationality and visualising the proportional split between Indian nationals and foreign nationals as a pie chart.

  • ConfirmedIndianNational and ConfirmedForeignNational were each summed using sum(na.rm = TRUE) to handle any missing values.
  • The two totals were combined into a named vector and passed to pie().
  • The chart clearly shows the proportional split: the vast majority of confirmed cases were among Indian nationals, with a much smaller share among foreign nationals.

What was the task?

To create a pie chart showing the distribution of confirmed COVID-19 cases by nationality, using the two nationality-specific variables in the dataset.

What did I do?

I computed the total confirmed cases for each nationality using sum(), combined the results into a named vector, and visualised the proportions with pie().

What were the results?

The pie chart revealed a substantial imbalance: confirmed cases among Indian nationals far outnumbered those among foreign nationals, reflecting both the demographic composition of India and likely differences in testing and reporting patterns.

What did I learn?

I learned how to aggregate numeric variables using sum() and how pie charts can effectively highlight proportional differences. I also reinforced the importance of summarising data before plotting to ensure the visualisation reflects meaningful totals rather than raw row values.

How will this help in future work?

Comparing groups by proportion is a common requirement in data reporting and research. This workflow — aggregate, combine, visualise — will be reusable whenever proportional comparisons need to be communicated clearly to a non-technical audience.

Question 3

Histogram of Recovery Numbers

▬ Histogram — hist()

The third task focused on understanding the distribution of recovery counts across all observations, using a histogram to reveal shape, spread, and skewness.

  • The histogram was generated from the Cured variable, showing how recovery numbers were distributed across all reported entries.
  • The distribution revealed the relative frequency of low, moderate, and high recovery counts.
  • The plot allowed identification of patterns such as right-skewness, where the majority of records had very few recoveries and a small number had high counts — consistent with the early pandemic period.

What was the task?

To create a histogram of the recovery variable (Cured) to visualise how recovery counts were distributed across all observations in the dataset.

What did I do?

After loading the dataset, I identified the Cured column and passed it to hist(), adding axis labels, a title, and a fill colour to improve readability.

What were the results?

The histogram showed that most observations had low recovery counts, with the distribution trailing off steeply to the right. This right-skewed shape reflects the early stage of the outbreak, when recoveries were still relatively rare events.

What did I learn?

I learned how to generate and interpret histograms in R. More broadly, the task reinforced that understanding distributional shape — including skewness and concentration — is a necessary step before choosing summary statistics or statistical models, since many methods assume normality.

How will this help in future work?

Histograms are a cornerstone of exploratory data analysis. Knowing how to read and produce them quickly will support outlier detection, model selection, and assumption checking in future statistical work.

Question 4

Line Chart of Total Confirmed Cases Over Time

— Line Chart — plot(type = "l")

The fourth task introduced time-series visualisation, requiring the date column to be properly formatted before plotting confirmed cases against time.

  • The Date column was converted from character to a proper Date object using as.Date() with the format "%d-%m-%Y", enabling correct chronological ordering on the x-axis.
  • A line chart was produced with plot(type = "l"), placing dates on the x-axis and total confirmed cases on the y-axis.
  • The resulting chart shows an upward trend with cases increasing gradually at first before rising more sharply, illustrating the accelerating pace of the outbreak over the recorded period.

What was the task?

To create a line chart showing how total confirmed COVID-19 cases changed over time, requiring the date column to be formatted correctly before plotting.

What did I do?

I converted the Date column to a standard Date type using as.Date() with the correct format string, verified the result with head(), and then produced a line chart using plot(type = "l").

What were the results?

The line chart displayed a clear upward trend in confirmed cases over the recorded period, with a gradual initial rise followed by a steeper increase — consistent with the exponential early growth pattern typical of infectious disease outbreaks.

What did I learn?

I learned that date formatting is a prerequisite for any time-series visualisation in R: without explicit conversion via as.Date(), R treats date strings as unordered factors, which would produce a meaningless or incorrectly ordered plot. I also gained confidence with plot()'s type parameter for selecting chart geometry.

How will this help in future work?

Time-series plotting is central to monitoring trends, forecasting, and reporting in any data-intensive field. Mastering date formatting and line chart construction in R will be directly applicable to future coursework, research, and professional analysis tasks involving temporal data.

Reflection

Unit 5 brought together four core visualisation types and demonstrated that no single chart is universally appropriate — the right choice depends on the nature of the variable and the question being asked. Bar charts suit categorical frequency comparisons; pie charts communicate proportional breakdowns between a small number of groups; histograms reveal distributional shape for continuous or count variables; and line charts track change over an ordered dimension such as time.

A consistent theme across all four tasks was the importance of data preparation before plotting. In Question 1, confirming column names with names() was essential before referencing State.UnionTerritory. In Question 2, aggregating with sum() meant the pie chart reflected meaningful totals rather than row-level values. In Question 4, the date formatting step was the difference between a chronologically correct line chart and a meaningless one. This reinforced a principle that runs through the whole module: the quality of a visualisation is determined upstream, in the preparation of the data that feeds it.

Overall, this activity built a reusable mental framework: identify the question, choose the appropriate chart type, prepare the data, produce the plot, and interpret the result in context. That sequence — not any individual function — is the transferable skill.

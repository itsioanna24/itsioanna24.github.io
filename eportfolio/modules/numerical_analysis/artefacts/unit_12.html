End of Module Presentation

Statistical Analysis of Type 2 Diabetes: Exploratory, Inferential and Predictive Modelling Using the Pima Indians Dataset

This end-of-module individual presentation synthesised the analytical skills developed across the full module — descriptive statistics, inferential testing, correlation, and regression — and applied them to a real-world clinical dataset. The Pima Indians Diabetes Dataset (768 adult women, UCI Machine Learning Repository) was used to explore risk factor distributions, test statistical associations, and develop predictive models for type 2 diabetes status.

The full presentation slides and supporting transcript are available for download below. The analysis was conducted entirely in R.

Section 3 — Regression Analysis

β Multiple Linear Regression  |  Logistic Regression  |  Model Comparison

Linear regression was used to predict glucose level from age, BMI, blood pressure, and pregnancies. The overall model was significant (F(4, 719) = 25.54, p < 0.001) but explained only 11.9% of glucose variance (adjusted R² = 0.119), indicating that glucose is determined by many factors beyond the four included predictors.

Bayes' Probability Activity

This activity applied fundamental probability concepts — marginal probabilities, conditional probabilities, and Bayes' theorem — to COVID-19 state-level reporting data from India (January–March 2020). The focus was on Kerala and Delhi, examining how recovery reports were distributed across states and using Bayes' theorem to reverse a conditional probability.

Step 1 — Marginal Probabilities

P(A) = count(A) / total

Approximately 1 in 5 reports in the dataset came from Kerala, and approximately 1 in 5 reports included at least one recovery. These marginal probabilities are computed from the overall composition of the dataset and make no assumptions about any relationship between state and recovery status.

Step 2 — Conditional Probabilities

P(B|A) = P(A ∩ B) / P(A) 
# Recovery reports per state

Conditional on a report coming from Kerala, there is a 36.54% chance it includes a recovery — substantially higher than the overall recovery rate of 20.37%. Delhi's conditional recovery rate is even higher at 40%. Both states show recovery rates well above the national average, suggesting that Kerala and Delhi — as among the earliest and most actively monitored states — had comparatively stronger recovery tracking during this period.

Step 3 — Applying Bayes' Theorem

P(A|B) = P(B|A) · P(A) / P(B)

Bayes' theorem reverses the condition. Instead of asking "given a report is from Kerala, what is the probability it includes a recovery?", we ask: "given a report includes a recovery, what is the probability it came from Kerala?"

Bayes' Theorem P(Kerala | Recovery) = P(Recovery | Kerala) × P(Kerala) / P(Recovery) 
# Bayes' theorem: P(Kerala | Recovery)
                            P_Kerala_given_Recovery <- (P_Recovery_given_Kerala * P_Kerala) / P_Recovery
                            
                            P_Kerala_given_Recovery
                            
                            # Equivalent direct calculation from counts
                            kerala_recovery / recovery_reports   # 19 / 55
Worked Calculation P(Kerala | Recovery) = (19/52 × 52/270) / (55/270) = 19/55 ≈ 0.3455
ProbabilityCalculationValue
P(Kerala | Recovery) 19 / 55 ≈ 0.3455 (34.55%)
⇧

Kerala is over-represented among recovery reports. While Kerala accounts for only 19.26% of all reports in the dataset, it contributes 34.55% of recovery reports — approximately 1.8 times its overall share. Bayes' theorem made this disproportionate contribution visible by conditioning on the evidence of a recovery.

The worked calculation shows an important simplification: when applied to count data, Bayes' theorem reduces to a straightforward ratio (19/55) because the total (270) cancels in the numerator and denominator. This is not always the case — in more complex applications, computing P(B) requires summing over all possible partitions of the sample space (the law of total probability). The algebraic simplicity here is a property of this particular two-state example, not of Bayes' theorem in general.
Prior vs posterior: In Bayesian language, P(Kerala) = 0.1926 is the prior probability — our belief about the probability of a Kerala report before observing any evidence. P(Kerala | Recovery) = 0.3455 is the posterior probability — our updated belief after learning that the report contains a recovery. The evidence (a recovery being present) has more than doubled our estimated probability that the report originated from Kerala.

What did I learn?

The most valuable conceptual insight was understanding what Bayes' theorem actually does: it updates a prior belief in light of new evidence. The prior (19.26%) and the posterior (34.55%) are both valid probabilities — they answer different questions. The prior describes the marginal composition of the dataset; the posterior describes the composition of a specific subset defined by the evidence. Conditional probabilities can reveal patterns that marginal proportions entirely conceal.

Why does the simplification work here?

In this example all counts come from the same total (270), so P(A) = count(A)/270 for every event. When Bayes' formula is expanded, the 270s cancel, reducing the result to a simple count ratio. In real Bayesian inference — for example, a spam classifier or a medical diagnostic model — P(B) must be computed from a weighted sum across all classes, and the cancellation does not occur.

How will this help in future work?

Bayes' theorem underlies Naive Bayes classifiers, Bayesian networks, and probabilistic inference in AI. Understanding how to move between prior and posterior probabilities, and how evidence updates beliefs, is foundational for any work in probabilistic machine learning, anomaly detection, or risk estimation. This activity grounded that abstract framework in a concrete, interpretable dataset.

Learning Outcome

Overall Reflection — Unit 12

Unit 12 brought together two artefacts that represent opposite ends of the analytical spectrum. The Bayes' probability activity was deliberately small in scale — a handful of counts, a single theorem, a single result — but conceptually dense, requiring a precise understanding of what probability means and how conditioning on evidence changes it. The end-of-module presentation was broad in scope, synthesising descriptive, inferential, and predictive techniques across an entire dataset and communicating the findings as a structured academic argument.

The Bayesian activity reinforced a principle that runs through all statistical reasoning: every probability is implicitly conditional on something. The marginal probability P(Kerala) = 19.26% and the posterior P(Kerala | Recovery) = 34.55% are both correct — they simply answer different questions. Learning to identify which question a probability answers, and to move between prior and posterior through Bayes' theorem, is foundational for probabilistic machine learning and AI systems where models must update their beliefs in light of new observations.

The presentation demanded a different kind of rigour: integrating multiple analytical layers into a coherent narrative with justified methodological choices at each step. The decision to use Welch's t-test despite non-normality (justified by sample size), to use Fisher's Exact Test instead of chi-square for small cell counts, to prefer Model 1 over Model 2 on parsimony and multicollinearity grounds — each of these reflected the kind of applied statistical judgement that the module as a whole has been building. The low adjusted R² in the linear regression (0.119) was also an honest result: acknowledging that a significant model can still explain only a modest fraction of variance is part of responsible quantitative reporting.

Taken together, Unit 12 demonstrated that statistical analysis is not a single technique but a toolkit that must be assembled thoughtfully for each problem — from the first frequency table to the final model evaluation — with decisions at every stage grounded in the properties of the data, the assumptions of the method, and the question being asked.

Peer Responses To My Post

Collaborative Discussion 1 - Peer Response 1

Discussion Forum

Reflection on this Artefact

The feedback on my discussion post provided a valuable extension to my original analysis, particularly by challenging my decision to prioritise agreement over other response categories. While the reviewer acknowledged that my redesign improved clarity and reduced cognitive load, they highlighted an important limitation: the potential loss of insight when “no opinion” responses are not considered.
This prompted me to reflect more critically on my assumption that neutrality has limited analytical value. I now recognise that “no opinion” responses can signal uncertainty, gaps in training, or ambiguity in professional roles—factors that are highly relevant in healthcare contexts. This feedback broadened my understanding of data interpretation, showing that what may initially appear as less actionable data can, in fact, reveal opportunities for intervention, such as targeted education or clearer guidelines.
Additionally, the suggestion to link statistical findings more explicitly to preventative actions strengthened my appreciation of how data presentation connects to real-world outcomes. While I focused on improving interpretability, I did not fully explore how the findings could inform system-level improvements, such as organisational support or policy changes.
Overall, this feedback helped me develop a more balanced perspective on data summarisation. In future work, I would aim to combine clear, agreement-focused summaries with a more explicit discussion of uncertainty, ensuring that important nuances are not overlooked while still maintaining clarity and usability for decision-makers.

Collaborative Discussion 1 - Peer Response 3

Discussion Forum

Reflection on this Artefact

This feedback affirmed the effectiveness of my redesign in improving clarity and reducing cognitive load, while also highlighting areas where greater precision could enhance communication. In particular, the suggestion to clarify the meaning of “% Agree (Range)” made me aware of a potential ambiguity in my presentation. Although the format was intended to simplify interpretation, I recognise that without explicit explanation, readers could misinterpret the range as a statistical measure (e.g., a confidence interval). This emphasised the importance of precision and clear labelling in data communication.
The feedback also encouraged me to think more carefully about how to represent uncertainty without compromising clarity. While I deliberately excluded “no opinion” and “disagree” categories to reduce clutter, the suggestion to include a subtle indication of uncertainty—such as a note in the caption—offered a balanced alternative. This approach would allow me to preserve simplicity while still acknowledging potentially important signals, such as unfamiliarity or unclear guidance.
Importantly, this response reinforced the idea that effective data presentation involves not only simplification, but also careful consideration of how information might be interpreted by different audiences. Small additions, such as footnotes or annotations, can significantly improve transparency without increasing cognitive load.
Overall, this feedback helped refine my understanding of how to balance clarity, precision, and completeness. In future work, I would ensure that all summary measures are explicitly defined and consider incorporating subtle cues to highlight uncertainty where relevant.

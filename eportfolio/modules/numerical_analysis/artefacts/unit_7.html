Data Activity 5

This activity moved from the COVID-19 dataset to a health dataset (Health_Data.sav), shifting focus from data wrangling and visualisation to descriptive statistics and inferential testing. The three questions covered measures of central tendency, five-number summaries, and a formal hypothesis test comparing systolic blood pressure across groups.

Question 1

Measures of Central Tendency for SBP, DBP, and Income

μ Mean  |  Median  |  Mode

Since R has no built-in mode() function for statistical mode, a custom function was written to identify the most frequently occurring value. A loop then applied all three measures across the three variables of interest.

SBP — the mean exceeds the median, suggesting a slight right skew driven by a minority of individuals with higher systolic readings. The average sits in the pre-hypertensive range.

DBP — mean and median are very close (82.77 vs 82), pointing to a near-symmetrical distribution. Values fall at the upper edge of the normal range.

Income — the median slightly exceeds the mean ($86,560.50 vs $85,194.49), indicating a mild left skew consistent with a small number of lower-income values pulling the mean downward. No extreme outliers are apparent.

What did I learn?

Writing a custom get_mode() function reinforced that R prioritises general-purpose tools over domain-specific shortcuts — and that understanding the underlying logic (tabulate + match) matters as much as knowing which function to call. I also deepened my understanding of skewness: when mean > median the distribution pulls right; when median > mean it pulls left. Seeing this play out differently across three variables in the same dataset made the concept concrete.

How will this help in future work?

Central tendency measures are the first diagnostic step in any statistical analysis. Comparing mean and median to detect skew before choosing between parametric and non-parametric methods will be a routine part of my analytical workflow going forward.

How will this help in future work?

Five-number summaries and boxplots are standard components of any descriptive statistics report. Being able to read and produce them fluently — and connect their features to distributional properties like skewness and outliers — will be essential for both academic assignments and professional data reporting.

Question 3

Hypothesis Test — SBP and Peptic Ulcer Status

t Welch's Independent Samples t-test — t.test()

Because systolic blood pressure (SBP) is a continuous variable and peptic ulcer status is binary (present / absent), the appropriate test is a Welch's independent samples t-test, which compares the mean SBP between the two groups without assuming equal variances.

Hypotheses

Null Hypothesis
H₀

There is no difference in mean systolic blood pressure between individuals with and without peptic ulcer.

Alternative Hypothesis
H₁

There is a difference in mean systolic blood pressure between individuals with and without peptic ulcer.

Results

StatisticValue
Mean SBP — Peptic ulcer present131.32 mmHg
Mean SBP — Peptic ulcer absent126.86 mmHg
Difference in means4.46 mmHg
t-value1.21
Degrees of freedom57.56
p-value0.23
95% Confidence Interval−2.89 to 11.80
◯

Fail to reject H₀ — At the 5% significance level (p = 0.23 > 0.05), there is insufficient evidence to conclude that mean SBP differs between individuals with and without peptic ulcer. The 95% confidence interval (−2.89 to 11.80) includes zero, confirming that the observed difference of 4.46 mmHg could plausibly arise by chance.

Although individuals with a peptic ulcer had a slightly higher mean SBP (131.32 vs 126.86 mmHg), the difference was not statistically significant. This does not mean no relationship exists — the sample may be underpowered to detect a small true effect — but on the basis of this data, the null hypothesis is retained. A larger sample or a more focused study design would be needed before drawing clinical conclusions.

What did I learn?

This task introduced the full cycle of inferential testing: selecting the appropriate test based on variable types, stating formal hypotheses, running the test in R, and interpreting the output in context. I learned that a non-significant result does not confirm the null hypothesis — it simply means the data do not provide sufficient evidence against it. I also reinforced the importance of the confidence interval as a complement to the p-value: a CI that spans zero tells the same story as p > 0.05, but also conveys the plausible range of the true effect.

Why Welch's t-test rather than Student's t-test?

Welch's t-test does not assume equal variances between groups — a more conservative and generally safer choice unless variance equality has been formally verified. R's t.test() applies Welch's correction by default, which is why the degrees of freedom (57.56) are non-integer.

How will this help in future work?

Hypothesis testing is central to research and data science. Knowing how to select the right test, formulate clear hypotheses, run the analysis in R, and interpret p-values and confidence intervals together will be directly applicable to any future study involving group comparisons — clinical, social, or otherwise.

Reflection

Unit 7 marked a significant step forward from descriptive to inferential statistics. The first two questions built a thorough portrait of the dataset's key variables — identifying skew direction, typical values, and spread — while the third question used those foundations to frame and test a formal research question. This progression mirrors real analytical workflows: you cannot test hypotheses responsibly without first understanding your data's distributional properties.

Writing a custom mode function was a reminder that R's ecosystem rewards users who understand the underlying logic rather than simply knowing which package to import. Equally, the need to use haven::read_sav() to load an SPSS file highlighted that real-world datasets rarely arrive in the same format — adaptability in data ingestion is a practical skill in itself.

The hypothesis testing question reinforced a key principle of statistical reasoning: statistical significance and practical significance are not the same thing. A 4.46 mmHg difference in mean SBP between groups could be clinically meaningful, yet the p-value of 0.23 means we cannot rule out chance with confidence. Holding both ideas simultaneously — the observed effect and the uncertainty around it — is the hallmark of careful statistical thinking.

Data Activity 8

This activity examined the relationship between systolic blood pressure (SBP) and diastolic blood pressure (DBP) in the Health_Data.sav dataset using Spearman's rank correlation and a scatter plot with a fitted trend line.

Method Selection

ρ Spearman's Rank Correlation — cor.test(method = "spearman")

Two correlation methods were available: Pearson's, which assumes both variables are normally distributed, and Spearman's rank correlation, which is non-parametric and makes no distributional assumptions. Because SBP was found to be non-normally distributed in Unit 8 (Shapiro–Wilk: W = 0.955, p < 0.001), Spearman's coefficient was the appropriate choice here.

Spearman's ρ measures the strength and direction of the monotonic relationship between two variables — that is, whether one variable tends to increase as the other increases, regardless of whether the relationship is perfectly linear. It is computed on the ranks of the data rather than the raw values, making it robust to outliers and non-normality.

Strong positive association — ρ = 0.80, p < 0.001. Individuals with higher systolic blood pressure tend strongly to also have higher diastolic blood pressure. The result is highly statistically significant, meaning the association is very unlikely to be due to chance.

A Spearman ρ of 0.80 falls in the strong range (typically defined as |ρ| > 0.70). The positive direction confirms that SBP and DBP move together — an expected physiological finding, since both measurements reflect the same underlying cardiovascular pressure system. The use = "complete.obs" argument ensures that any rows with missing values in either variable are excluded pairwise, preventing missing data from distorting the correlation estimate.

Scatter Plot with Trend Line



The scatter plot shows a clear upward trend: as SBP increases along the x-axis, DBP values rise correspondingly on the y-axis. The data points cluster reasonably closely around the fitted regression line, with some natural spread, which is consistent with a strong but not perfect correlation. The pch = 16 argument renders filled circles, which are easier to read than the default open circles when many points overlap. The trend line was fitted using OLS (lm()), which shows the linear component of the relationship — complementing rather than replacing the Spearman analysis, which captures the full monotonic association.

Why Spearman rather than Pearson?

Pearson's r measures linear association and assumes both variables are normally distributed. Because SBP was found to violate normality in the Shapiro–Wilk test conducted in Unit 8, using Pearson's here would have been methodologically inconsistent. Spearman's ρ operates on ranks, bypassing the normality requirement entirely and remaining valid regardless of the distributional shape of either variable.

What did I learn?

Beyond confirming that SBP and DBP are strongly associated, the most important lesson was that correlation analysis involves two complementary layers: the numerical coefficient and the visual scatter plot. The coefficient alone (0.80) tells you the strength and direction, but the plot reveals the shape of the relationship — whether it is linear, curved, or driven by outliers. Neither alone is sufficient for a complete analysis.

How will this help in future work?

Correlation analysis is a core step in feature selection for machine learning models — highly correlated predictors introduce redundancy and can destabilise model estimates. Understanding how to measure and visualise associations between continuous variables, and how to choose the right correlation method based on distributional properties, will be directly applicable to any future predictive modelling or feature engineering task.

Overall Reflection

Unit 10 introduced correlation analysis as a tool for quantifying relationships between continuous variables, and reinforced a principle that has run through the last several units: the choice of statistical method must follow from the properties of the data. The decision to use Spearman rather than Pearson was not a preference — it was a direct consequence of the normality check carried out in Unit 8, demonstrating how earlier analytical steps inform later ones.

The strong result (ρ = 0.80) was expected from a physiological standpoint — SBP and DBP both reflect arterial pressure — but confirming it statistically is still meaningful. It establishes an empirical basis for treating these two variables as partly redundant in any downstream model, which has practical implications for feature selection and multicollinearity management in regression or machine learning contexts.

Finally, combining cor.test() with plot() and abline() demonstrated that R's base graphics, while simpler than ggplot2, remain effective for exploratory analysis where speed and clarity matter more than publication polish.

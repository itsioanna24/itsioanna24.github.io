Reflection on this Artefact

This artefact reflects my engagement with the limitations of common statistical reporting practices, particularly confidence intervals and p-values. Writing this post marked a shift in my thinking from applying statistical tools to critically evaluating their appropriateness in context.
A key insight was recognising that statistical measures can create a false sense of certainty when underlying assumptions are weak or when data quality issues dominate. By questioning the routine use of confidence intervals, I demonstrated a growing awareness that statistical outputs are not inherently meaningful unless their assumptions are valid. This reflects an important development in my ability to think beyond standard practices and consider the reliability of results.
I also critically examined the use of p-values, highlighting their limitations in supporting decision-making. This reinforced my understanding that statistical significance does not equate to practical importance, and that overreliance on p-values can lead to superficial interpretations. Engaging with this debate helped me align my thinking with contemporary critiques of null hypothesis significance testing.
Another important aspect of this task was considering the audience. I recognised that statistical outputs are often interpreted by non-experts, which increases the responsibility to present results in a way that is both accurate and meaningful. This strengthened my view that effect sizes, assumptions, and contextual uncertainty should be prioritised over isolated metrics.
Overall, this post reflects my development towards a more critical, reflective, and context-aware approach to statistical communication.

Data Activity 7

This activity returned to the COVID-19 India dataset (January–March 2020) to explore relationships between categorical variables using cross-tabulation. Two tasks were completed: examining how COVID-19 case severity varied across geographical regions, and exploring the relationship between case severity and recovery status.

Data Preparation

Before cross-tabulation could be performed, three new categorical variables had to be engineered from the raw dataset. This required importing the dataset, inspecting its structure, and applying conditional transformations using dplyr::mutate() with case_when().

No Cases entries unsurprisingly show a 100% "Not Recovered" rate since no confirmed cases means no recorded recoveries.

Low-severity cases show a predominantly "Not Recovered" pattern (77%), with only 23% classified as recovered. This is consistent with the early stage of the outbreak when limited treatment pathways and short observation windows meant few cases had been formally closed as recovered.

Medium-severity cases show a strikingly different pattern: 47% were classified as recovered — nearly as many as "Not Recovered". This finding is counterintuitive at first glance but may reflect that medium-severity cases required longer hospitalisation and therefore had more complete follow-up data, or that higher-severity records were from later in the period when recovery tracking had improved.

No high-severity cases were observed in this dataset, as confirmed above.
Methodological note: These proportional tables use prop.table(..., margin = 1), meaning each row sums to 1. This gives the conditional distribution of the column variable given the row category — the appropriate choice when asking "within each severity level, what proportion recovered?" Using margin = 2 (column proportions) or no margin (overall proportions) would answer different questions and should not be confused with this approach.

What did I learn?

This task demonstrated that cross-tabulation is not merely a counting exercise — the choice between raw counts, row proportions, column proportions, and overall proportions fundamentally changes what question is being answered. Using margin = 1 for row proportions was the correct choice here because both tasks asked about the conditional distribution of one variable within each category of another. I also deepened my understanding of dplyr::mutate() with case_when() as a powerful tool for creating multiple categorical variables from a single transformation pipeline — a pattern that is far cleaner than repeated ifelse() calls.

What surprised you?

The most unexpected result was that medium-severity cases had a substantially higher recovery proportion (47%) than low-severity cases (23%). This challenged the intuition that higher severity should correlate with worse outcomes, and prompted thinking about confounding factors — such as reporting completeness, follow-up duration, and temporal trends — that could explain the pattern. It was a useful reminder that descriptive statistics reveal patterns but do not explain them.

How will this help in future work?

Cross-tabulation and proportional tables are foundational tools in exploratory data analysis, feature engineering, and model diagnostics. Being comfortable with table(), prop.table(), and the distinction between margin types will support any future work involving categorical data — from public health research to machine learning feature analysis.

Reflection

Unit 9 introduced cross-tabulation as a bridge between data transformation and statistical exploration. The real analytical work happened before the tables were even produced — in the decisions about how to group states into regions, where to draw the case severity thresholds, and how to define a binary recovery status from a continuous cured count. These choices are not neutral: different boundaries would produce different tables and potentially different conclusions.

The case_when() function proved to be a significant upgrade over nested ifelse() calls for multi-category variable creation. Its explicit, readable structure makes the classification logic self-documenting — an important quality in reproducible analysis where code may be revisited or reviewed by others.

The most intellectually valuable outcome was encountering a result that required explanation — the higher recovery rate among medium-severity cases. Descriptive analysis can surface anomalies, but understanding them requires contextual knowledge about how the data were collected. This is the point where statistical literacy and domain knowledge must work together, and it is a principle that extends well beyond this dataset.

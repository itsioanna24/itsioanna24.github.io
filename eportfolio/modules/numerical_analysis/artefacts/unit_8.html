Question 3

SBP Across Occupational Groups

≈ Shapiro–Wilk → Kruskal–Wallis Test

Before selecting a test, the normality of systolic blood pressure was formally assessed. Since comparing more than two groups is required, the choice was between a one-way ANOVA (parametric, assumes normality) and the Kruskal–Wallis test (non-parametric alternative).

TestStatisticp-valueDecision
Shapiro–Wilk (normality of SBP) W = 0.955 < 0.001 Normality violated → use non-parametric test
Kruskal–Wallis (SBP across occupations) χ²(3) = 0.78 0.8545 Fail to reject H₀
◯

Fail to reject H₀ — p = 0.8545 > 0.05. There is no statistically significant difference in systolic blood pressure across occupational groups in this dataset. The very small χ² value (0.78) relative to 3 degrees of freedom confirms negligible between-group variation.

What did I learn?

This activity reinforced that test selection is not arbitrary — it follows directly from the properties of the data. The Shapiro–Wilk result for SBP (p < 0.001) made the choice of Kruskal–Wallis necessary; using ANOVA without checking normality first would have been a methodological error. I also learned to use dplyr's group_by() %>% summarise() pipeline to compute group-level statistics efficiently — a pattern that will recur throughout any multi-group analysis.

How will this help in future work?

Health and social science datasets frequently violate normality assumptions. Knowing when to step down to a non-parametric method — and how to justify that choice with a formal test — is an essential skill for any evidence-based analysis role.

Scenario-Based Exercise — Comparing Training Vendors

An HR organisation hired three training vendors, each delivering a one-week programme to a separate employee group. Assuming equal pre-training efficiency across groups, the task was to compare post-training efficiency scores and determine whether vendor differences were statistically significant at the 95% confidence level.

The problem statement described 8 employees per group, but only 7 observations were provided per vendor. The analysis proceeds with the available 7 scores per group.

Assumption Checking

Normality of Residuals

Shapiro–Wilk: W = 0.977, p = 0.877

p > 0.05 — no evidence of departure from normality. The Q-Q plot confirms residuals follow the theoretical normal line closely. Normality assumption satisfied.

Homogeneity of Variance

Levene's Test: F(2, 18) = 0.216, p = 0.808

p > 0.05 — no evidence of unequal variances across vendor groups. Homogeneity of variance assumption satisfied. ANOVA results are valid and reliable.

Post-Hoc Analysis — Tukey's HSD

T Tukey's Honest Significant Difference

ANOVA indicates that at least one mean differs, but does not identify which pairs. Tukey's HSD test was conducted to locate the specific group differences.

ComparisonMean Difference95% CIAdjusted p-valueSignificant?
Vendor 2 − Vendor 1 +6.29 (−3.52, 16.10) 0.257 No
Vendor 3 − Vendor 1 −17.00 (−26.81, −7.19) < 0.001 Yes
Vendor 3 − Vendor 2 −23.29 (−33.10, −13.48) < 0.001 Yes
Vendor 2 vs Vendor 1: The 6.29-point mean difference is not statistically significant (p = 0.257); the CI includes zero. Vendors 1 and 2 cannot be statistically distinguished on this evidence alone.

Vendor 3 vs Vendor 1: Vendor 3 scores 17 points lower on average (p < 0.001). The CI excludes zero, providing strong evidence of inferior performance.

Vendor 3 vs Vendor 2: The largest difference in the analysis — Vendor 3 scores 23.29 points below Vendor 2 (p < 0.001). This is the most decisive comparison.

Conclusion

At the 95% confidence level, training vendor significantly affects post-training efficiency. Vendor 3 performs substantially and significantly worse than both Vendor 1 and Vendor 2, while no statistically significant difference exists between Vendors 1 and 2. Although Vendor 2 is not statistically superior to Vendor 1, it achieves the highest mean efficiency score (51.00) and moderate variability — making it the strongest overall recommendation based on both descriptive evidence and statistical inference.

Overall Reflection

Unit 8 covered the full spectrum from non-parametric group comparisons to parametric ANOVA — and the crucial skill linking them: knowing which to use and why. Both artefacts reinforced the same decision-making sequence: inspect the data, check assumptions, choose the appropriate test, run it, and interpret the result in context.

The Wilcoxon and Kruskal–Wallis tests in Data Activity 6 demonstrated that "non-parametric" does not mean inferior — it means making fewer assumptions, which is often the correct choice. Crucially, using the Shapiro–Wilk test to motivate the switch from ANOVA to Kruskal–Wallis showed that assumption checking is not a formality but an active part of the analysis.

The scenario-based exercise introduced a complete ANOVA workflow — descriptive analysis, formal hypothesis testing, assumption verification (normality and homogeneity of variance), and post-hoc pairwise comparisons via Tukey's HSD. The most important lesson from the Tukey results was that a significant overall ANOVA does not mean every pair of groups differs: Vendors 1 and 2 were indistinguishable despite the ANOVA flagging a global effect. Post-hoc testing is therefore not optional — it is where the actionable conclusions are actually located.

Reflections

A reflective piece on my contribution, growth and learning throughout the Numerical Analysis module

← Back to Numerical Analysis module

Introduction

This reflective piece reviews my learning and professional development throughout the Numerical Analysis module, using the Four F's framework (Facts, Feelings, Findings and Future) as an active reviewing tool rather than a rigid structure (The University of Edinburgh, 2024). The framework enables systematic reflection on what I did, how I experienced the learning process, what I learned, and how this learning will inform my future academic and professional practice.

The reflection is organised into four categories that mirror how my learning progressed through the module, and draws explicitly on artefacts created throughout the 12 units.

R Workflow, IT & Digital Skills, Problem-Solving, and Time Management

Facts

My confidence in R was supported by prior coding experience, but early module tasks still exposed gaps in my data workflow. In Data Activities 1 and 2, I moved from basic descriptive statistics (mean, median, mode, spread measures) to R data structures and file-based work. My first real difficulty was not coding itself but managing the dataset correctly — including understanding how comma-separated data is parsed and how R interprets columns after import (Bader and Leuzinger, 2024).

Feelings

Emotionally, this phase was a mix of confidence and friction: confidence because I could write code, and friction because the dataset did not behave as expected. When outputs were not what I anticipated, I initially felt frustrated and worried that I was missing something basic. This was especially true when variables produced unexpected results — which forced me to confront the difference between writing code and managing data reliably.

Findings

The key finding was that data handling discipline is foundational: importing data correctly, checking structure, and confirming variable types prevents analytical mistakes further down the pipeline. Across Data Activities 2 and 3, I learned to slow down and repeatedly verify — checking names(), head(), str(), and row counts before creating indicators. Creating binary variables such as has_recovery and the deaths indicator strengthened my ability to translate raw numeric fields into analytically meaningful features. In DA 3, converting indicators to factors improved interpretability and set me up for clearer summaries and later visualisation. I also learned that "simple" steps like table() and prop.table() are not just descriptive — they are diagnostic tools that reveal how data is distributed before deeper analysis.

Time management developed most clearly through the way I planned and built the End of Module assignments progressively. Instead of treating the EOM work as a final-week activity, I recorded reflections and progress after each unit. This helped me distribute effort, reduce last-minute risk, and create a coherent workflow from unit learning into final deliverables.

Future

As a result, I now approach new datasets with a deliberate workflow: import → inspect → clean/convert types → engineer features → summarise → visualise → test/model. I will apply this directly in future AI projects — for instance, prediction tasks — by building reproducible R scripts and structured project directories so that data issues do not undermine modelling outcomes (Bader and Leuzinger, 2024).

Numeracy, Critical Thinking and Research Skills: Statistical Inference and Interpretation

Facts

As the module advanced from descriptive statistics into inferential analysis, I applied a range of statistical methods across multiple data activities. This included using histograms to explore distributions, performing correlation analysis between continuous variables, fitting and diagnosing simple linear regression models, selecting parametric or non-parametric tests based on normality assessment, and interpreting confidence intervals from ANOVA post-hoc comparisons in the scenario-based exercise.

Feelings

Emotionally, this was the hardest part of the module. Even when I could run tests in R, I often felt uncertain about whether I was selecting the most appropriate method. The difficulty was not computing p-values or producing outputs, but evaluating assumptions, matching the test to the question, and then interpreting results responsibly. This uncertainty became more visible when results were non-significant or when distributional assumptions were violated. Tutor feedback reinforced this: the work was good, but I needed to increase the criticality of my discussions and interpretations — highlighting that correct calculations alone are not sufficient.

Findings

The main finding was that inference is a decision process. I learned to treat normality tests and assumption checks as central rather than optional, and to justify choices clearly (Ranganathan, 2021; Vrbin, 2022). Visual exploration using histograms helped me recognise skewness and informed later test selection, while correlation analysis required careful interpretation beyond numerical strength alone. Regression modelling further reinforced this — examining coefficients, confidence intervals, and diagnostic plots highlighted the importance of validating assumptions rather than relying solely on statistical significance.

Interpreting 95% confidence intervals in the scenario-based exercise strengthened my ability to focus on estimation precision and uncertainty rather than dichotomous "significant/non-significant" conclusions. This perspective was strongly shaped by engagement with methodological literature on distinguishing statistical significance from practical importance (Schober, Bossers and Schwarte, 2018; Tijssen, 2021).

Future

Going forward, I will apply this skill directly in AI contexts by treating assumption checking and diagnostics as part of model validity, reporting uncertainty clearly using confidence intervals where applicable, and avoiding "significance chasing" when interpreting results. In modelling projects — especially observational health data — I will use regression and diagnostic reasoning as part of responsible inference and reporting (Zapf, Wiessner and König, 2024).

Communication, Interpersonal Skills and Ethical Awareness

Facts

I produced multiple summaries and visualisations across Data Activity 4 (bar chart, pie chart, histogram, line chart) and later tasks (scatter plots with trend lines). I also engaged in two collaborative discussions: one centred on redesigning and interpreting a complex table linked to Brown (1994), and another centred on the interpretation of confidence intervals and p-values.

Feelings

Emotionally, I initially treated tables and plots as requirements rather than as analytical reasoning tools. Peer engagement challenged this. I felt a productive tension between wanting to simplify outputs for clarity and recognising that simplification can erase important uncertainty. This was particularly relevant when peers questioned what should be retained or explained for interpretability.

Findings

My key learning from Collaborative Discussion 1 was not merely "simplify tables", but simplify with transparency and retain meaningful uncertainty. In my summary, I acknowledged peer agreement that thematic aggregation improves interpretability for decision-making audiences. However, peers raised a crucial point: "no opinion" responses may represent meaningful ambiguity rather than noise. By framing good presentation as a balance between reducing complexity and responsibly communicating uncertainty, I realised that how we present statistics is a direct ethical choice that shapes both interpretation and downstream decisions (Brown, 1994).

Collaborative Discussion 2 deepened my ethical awareness regarding the uncritical reporting of p-values and confidence intervals. Peer engagement reinforced that statistical outputs are conditional tools shaped by assumptions, data quality, and context (Tijssen, 2021). Interpersonally, I also developed by evaluating others' work — writing peer responses required me to articulate what was clear, what was missing, and how interpretation could be strengthened, skills that map directly to professional review processes in analytics teams.

Future

In future assignments and professional projects, I will include assumptions and limitations statements alongside tests and models, especially for observational data (Zapf, Wiessner and König, 2024). When creating tables and figures, I will prioritise interpretability without removing uncertainty, using captions or notes to clarify what neutrality or missingness means rather than suppressing it.

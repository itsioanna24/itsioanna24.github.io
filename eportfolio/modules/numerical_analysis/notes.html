Unit 1 – Types and Sources of Data and an Introduction to R

Lecturecast Notes

  • Statistics rely heavily on the calculations of numbers, but also on how the numbers are chosen and how the statistics are interpreted. Interpretations can sometimes be problematic.
  • With inferential statistics, we take a sample — a small subset of a larger dataset — and use it to draw inferences about the population as a whole.
  • Before conducting a statistical analysis, variables must be measured. Exactly how depends on the type of variable involved.

Measures of Central Tendency

  • Mean: the sum of all values divided by the count of values. Describes the centre of a frequency distribution. Often used interchangeably with "average".
  • Median: the middle number in an ordered list of values.
  • Mode: the most frequently occurring value.

Measures of Variability

  • Variability refers to how "spread out" a group of scores is.
  • Range: the simplest measure — highest score minus lowest score.
  • Interquartile Range (IQR): the 75th percentile minus the 25th percentile.
  • Variance: the average squared difference of each score from the mean.
  • Standard Deviation: the square root of the variance.

Unit 2 – Data Analysis and Programming in R

Lecturecast Notes

R Data Structures

  • Vector: an object containing elements of the same data type. Defined using the c() function to concatenate elements.
  • Matrix: a collection of elements of the same data type (usually integers or real numbers) arranged in a two-dimensional rectangular or square format.
  • Limitation of vectors and matrices: they can only contain one data type — no mixing of integers and characters.
  • List: an object that allows a combination of different data types in a single object.
  • Data Frame: a special case of a list. The most commonly used data structure for statistical analysis.

Unit 6 – Statistical Inference and Hypothesis Testing

Lecturecast Notes

Statistical Inference

  • Statistical inference uses probability to support decision-making under uncertainty.
  • Sample data provides an estimate of a population parameter, not its exact value.
  • A confidence interval expresses the degree of closeness between sample and population values. A 95% confidence level means we are 95% confident the sample values correspond to population values, with a 5% chance of error.

Hypothesis Testing

  • A hypothesis is a supposition made from observation, used to guide data collection.
  • Null hypothesis (H₀): states there is no effect in the underlying population.
  • Alternative hypothesis (H₁ / Hₐ): the opposite — states that groups are different.

Possible Outcomes When Testing H₀

State of H₀Test DecisionOutcome
TrueRejectedType I Error (α)
FalseNot rejectedType II Error (β)
TrueNot rejectedCorrect
FalseRejectedCorrect

The p-value

  • Represents the probability that the observed results occurred purely by chance.
  • 0.05 > p > 0.01 → Significant
  • p < 0.01 → Highly significant
  • p < 0.001 or 0.005 → Very highly significant
  • The level of significance (α) is the threshold strength of evidence required to reject H₀.

Unit 7 – Descriptive Statistics and Hypothesis Testing in R

Lecturecast Notes

t-Tests

Test TypePurposeHypotheses
One-Sample t-Test Compares the mean of a sample to a constant or hypothetical population mean (μ₀). H₀: Population mean = μ₀  |  Hₐ: Population mean ≠ μ₀
Two-Sample t-Test Compares the means of two independent samples. H₀: μ₁ = μ₂  |  Hₐ: μ₁ ≠ μ₂
Paired t-Test Compares means of two related samples (e.g. before/after measurements on the same subjects). H₀: means are equal  |  Hₐ: means are not equal

Normality Testing

  • The Shapiro–Wilk test is used to assess whether data are normally distributed.
  • H₀: the data are normally distributed.
  • R command: shapiro.test(data$variable)

ANOVA (Analysis of Variance)

  • Used to compare the means of three or more independent groups.
  • One-Way ANOVA: involves one categorical variable (with >2 levels) and one quantitative variable.
  • The t-test compares two groups; ANOVA extends this to more than two.

Unit 8 – Non-Parametric Tests and ANOVA in R

Lecturecast Notes

Non-Parametric Tests

TestKey Characteristics
Mann-Whitney U Test
  • Compares two independent groups.
  • Compares the medians of two samples.
  • Dependent variable: ordinal or continuous scale; independent variable: two independent categorical groups.
  • Data can be skewed. Equivalent to a two-sample Wilcoxon rank-sum test.
Wilcoxon Signed Ranks Test
  • Used for two measures from the same cases when data are not normally distributed, or when sample size is large.
  • Key difference from Mann-Whitney U: tests two dependent (paired) samples, not independent ones.
  • Use the Sign test when differences have a direction but the absolute quantity is not precise and scores are paired.
Kruskal-Wallis Test
  • Uses ranks of the data rather than raw values.
  • Extends the Mann-Whitney U test to more than two groups.
  • Appropriate when normality is violated for interval-scaled dependent variables.
  • H₀: the mean ranks of all groups are the same.

Unit 10 – Correlation Analysis in R

Lecturecast Notes

Correlation

  • Correlation is a statistical measurement of the relationship between two variables.
  • Independent variable: can be controlled or manipulated. Dependent variable: cannot be controlled; its values are predicted from the independent variable.
  • Best visualised using a scatter plot.
  • The correlation coefficient (r) ranges from −1 to +1 and measures the strength and direction of a linear relationship.

Types of Correlation

Typer valueMeaning
Exact positive linear+1Both variables increase together perfectly
Strong positiveClose to +1Both variables tend to increase together
Exact negative linear−1One increases as the other decreases perfectly
Strong negativeClose to −1One tends to increase as the other decreases
Poor / no correlationClose to 0No clear linear relationship

Regression

  • Regression analysis identifies which variables have a real impact on an outcome.
  • Linear regression finds the best-fitting straight line through data showing a linear pattern.
  • Accuracy depends on the linear relationship between variables — measured by the correlation coefficient.
  • Pearson's Correlation Coefficient is standardised covariance and only measures linear relationships.

Unit 11 – Simple Linear Regression in R

Lecturecast Notes

Importance of Variable Direction

  • Correlation is not causation. Variables can appear correlated while being entirely unrelated (e.g. ice cream sales and murder rates in New York, 2009).
  • In correlation, it does not matter which variable is dependent and which is independent.
  • In regression, direction matters — the line predicting X from Y is not the same as the line predicting Y from X.

R-values and R²

  • Pearson correlation coefficient r ranges from −1 to +1.
  • Linear regression quantifies goodness of fit as R².
  • The square of r from correlation equals R² from regression.

Residuals

  • The residual is the distance between the observed y value and the calculated (predicted) y value.
  • A best-fitting line minimises the residuals across all data points.
  • Larger residuals indicate poorer model fit at those points.

Unit 12 – Bayes' Theorem and Probability

Lecturecast Notes

Bayesian Data Analysis

  • A Bayesian analysis consists of the application of Bayes' theorem to a model and a dataset.
  • The model design is flexible and can be custom-tailored for each application.

Four Steps of Bayesian Data Analysis

  1. Design a model for the data.
  2. Specify a prior distribution for all model parameters.
  3. Calculate the posterior probability of model parameters based on the model and dataset.
  4. Summarise and visualise the posterior distribution.

Bayes' Theorem

  • Used to take a prior probability, update it with data (the likelihood), and obtain a posterior probability.

Frequentist vs Bayesian Paradigms

ParadigmWhat is fixed?What is uncertain?Notation
Frequentist Parameters Data (repeatable) P(data | parameters)
Bayesian Observed data Parameters P(parameters | data)
  • Frequentist statistics is about repeatability and gathering more data. Concepts previously covered in this module — p-values and confidence intervals — belong to this paradigm.
  • Bayesian inference treats the observed data as fixed and expresses uncertainty about the model parameters themselves.

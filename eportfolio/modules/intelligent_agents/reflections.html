Reflections

A reflective piece on my contribution, growth and learning throughout the Intelligent Agents module

← Back to Intelligent Agents module

Introduction

This reflection adopts Rolfe, Freshwater and Jasper’s (2001) What? – So What? – Now What? framework to critically examine my learning across the Intelligent Agents module. It addresses my engagement with the collaborative discussions, individual activities, team design project, and individual implementation, and reflects on how these four strands shaped my understanding of intelligent agent systems and my development as a collaborative practitioner.

What?

My engagement with the module consisted of four interlinked strands. First, three collaborative discussions — on the strengths and verification challenges of agent-based systems, on agent communication languages and speech-act theory, and on ethical risks in deep learning and generative AI — where I posted initial contributions, responded to peers and synthesised the summary post. Second, three individual e-portfolio activities: constructing a KQML/KIF dialogue between a procurement and a warehouse agent, producing constituency parse trees for three sentences, and analysing deep learning in autonomous vehicles with the 2018 Uber fatality as a case study. Third, a team design proposal project where I worked with three peers across eight meetings to deliver a design proposal for an Academic Research Online System using BDI agents coordinated through a blackboard; my role was hybrid, coordinating the initial structure of the document, authoring the System Requirements section across three iterations, writing the conclusion, and supporting consolidation. Fourth, the individual project, where I implemented the system our team had proposed — writing the complete Python codebase including the four specialised agents, the blackboard environment, the adaptive ProcessingAgent logic, and a 24-test validation suite — and then delivered a ten-slide presentation walking through the code and the system’s behaviour.

So What?

The team project and group dynamics

The emotional arc of the module moved through three phases. At the start of the team project I felt genuine anxiety because this was my first team project on the programme and the teams had been randomly allocated: I did not know whether I would work well with classmates I had never met. The first meetings quickly turned that anxiety into relief — communication was open, members listened and offered constructive feedback, and responsibilities were allocated collaboratively. However, my initial anxiety meant that in the early meetings I was more reserved than I would typically be, contributing less proactively until I became more comfortable with the group dynamic. Through Tuckman’s (1965) model of group development, Group E moved through forming and storming unusually smoothly, which I attribute to the early investment in shared documentation and explicit role allocation.

How the earlier strands prepared me

The earlier strands of the module had quietly prepared me for the project work, though I did not experience this as preparation at the time. The three discussions shifted my positions in ways I could not have reached alone — from a technical view of verification to a socio-technical one, from ACL mechanics to shared semantics, from post-hoc ethics to lifecycle governance. The three activities reinforced this in a similar way: the KQML/KIF dialogues made structured coordination tangible before I had to specify it; the parse-tree activity calibrated my expectations about rule-based language; and the Deep Learning activity — analysing the Uber case through the perception–decision–action lens — sharpened my view of the symbolic-versus-learning trade-off I would later confront in the ProcessingAgent.

The individual project

The individual project produced a sharper register: stress. The difficulty was not technical ambition but the pressure of translating into working code the literature I had read for the team proposal (Nii, 1986; Bratman, Israel and Pollack, 1988; Brooks, 1991; Rao and Georgeff, 1995), while keeping the implementation aligned with what the team had proposed. Any divergence between code and the proposal would undermine the coherence of my submission. What dissolved the stress was the act of building the system itself. Writing the perceive–deliberate–act cycle across the four agents, wiring the blackboard as the sole communication channel, and watching the logs show the operational cycle of the system in action demonstrated that my understanding was operational, not just conceptual. Schön’s (1992) distinction between reflection-in-action and reflection-on-action captures it: the stress converted reflection-on what I had read into reflection-in what I was building.

Now What?

The most important change I will carry forward is confidence in my own technical contribution. Having implemented an end-to-end BDI Multi-Agent System, rather than merely specified one, I now trust that my understanding of agent architectures is operational and not just conceptual. The stress I felt translating the literature into working code turned out to be productive: it was the mechanism that forced me to resolve ambiguities I had been able to leave open in the team proposal. In future projects, I will expect a similar phase of pressure when moving from specification to implementation, and treat it as a signal of learning rather than a sign that something is wrong.

Practically, three changes follow. First, I will treat requirements documents in agent or ML projects as living artefacts, maintaining version-controlled documentation alongside code repositories to ensure alignment between evolving system design and implementation (Wooldridge, 2009; Sommerville, 2016). Second, I will use the BDI lens — perceive–deliberate–act, with explicit intention reconsideration — as a practical design heuristic, applying it explicitly when structuring agent-based or LLM-driven systems in future projects. Implementing this cycle in code, not just writing about it, is what gave me the confidence to use it as a heuristic. Third, I will carry the lifecycle view of AI ethics surfaced in Collaborative Discussion 3 into professional practice, treating model cards, dataset documentation, and continuous auditing as default elements of any AI-adjacent specification I contribute to, rather than post-deployment afterthoughts (Mitchell et al., 2019; Raji et al., 2020; Gebru et al., 2021).

The honest area I still need to develop is entering new team environments without the initial anxiety that shaped my first weeks in Group E. Although that anxiety dissolved quickly once I met my teammates, it nonetheless made me less willing to propose ideas in the early meetings than I might otherwise have been. In future group work I intend to treat the forming stage as an opportunity to contribute actively rather than a period to get through, drawing on Tuckman’s (1965) observation that early engagement shapes later group dynamics.

Overall, this module has shifted my perspective from understanding intelligent agents as theoretical constructs to approaching them as practical, implementable systems embedded within collaborative and ethically responsible development processes.

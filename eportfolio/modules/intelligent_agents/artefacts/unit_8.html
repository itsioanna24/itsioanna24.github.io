Learning Outcomes

Through this activity, I gained a clearer understanding of how constituency parsing represents the structure of sentences by organising words into hierarchical phrase groups. By constructing parse trees for the example sentences, I learned how sentences can be decomposed into components such as noun phrases (NP) and verb phrases (VP), which form the basis for computational language analysis.

The syntactic structures produced for the first two sentences are illustrated in Figures 1 and 2, where the hierarchical organisation of the subject noun phrase and verb phrase can be clearly observed. However, the sentence “The man saw tha dog with the telescope”, highlights an important limitation of syntactic analysis. As shown in Figure 3, the attachment of the prepositional phrase can create structural ambiguity, resulting in different possible interpretations of the sentence. This illustrates a key challenge in natural language processing (NLP), as intelligent systems must determine the most appropriate syntactic interpretation in order to correctly infer meaning.

While constituency parsing provides a structured representation of sentence syntax, it does not always resolve semantic ambiguity on its own, which means additional contextual or probabilistic techniques are often required in real-world intelligent systems (Bird, Ewan Klein and Loper, 2009). From the perspective of intelligent agent systems, syntactic parsing enables agent to process and reason about human language inputs, supporting more effective communication and interaction between humans and intelligent systems (Wooldridge, 2009; Russell and Norvig, 2022). This activity helped me appreciate how linguistic structure forms a foundation for the development of intelligent systems capable of understanding and responding to natural language. This directly supports the development and evaluation of intelligent system techniques for real-world language understanding, which is a key learning outcome of the module.

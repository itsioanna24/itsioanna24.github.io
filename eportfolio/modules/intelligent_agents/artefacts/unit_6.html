Creating Agent Dialogues Activity

In this scenario, Alice is a procurement agent responsible for ordering products, while Bob is a warehouse management agent that maintains information about current stock levels. Alice communicates with Bob using KQML performatives to request information, while the content of the messages is expressed using KIF (Knowledge Interchange Format).

1. Alice asks Bob for the available stock of 50-inch televisions

(kqmlmsg
:performative ask-one
:sender Alice
: receiver Bob
:language KIF
:ontology warehouse-stock
:content (stock-level (product television-50inch) ?quantity)
)

Explanation:

  • ask-one indicates Alice is requesting a single answer.
  • ?quantity represents a variable whose value Bob will return.
  • The ontology specifies that both agents understand the warehouse stock domain.


2. Bob replies with available stock

(kqmlmsg
:performative tell
:sender Bob
: receiver Alice
:language KIF
:ontology warehouse-stock
:content (= (stock-level (product television-50inch)) 120)
)

Explanation:

  • Bob informs Alice that 120 units of 50-inch televisions are currently available in the warehouse.


3. Alice asks about the number of HDMI ports

(kqmlmsg
:performative ask-one
:sender Alice
: receiver Bob
:language KIF
:ontology warehouse-stock
:content (hdmi-ports (product television-50inch) ?ports)
)

Explanation:

  • Alice now queries the technical specification of the product to determine how many HDMI slots the televisions have.


3. Bob responds with the HDMI slot information

(kqmlmsg
:performative tell
:sender Bob
: receiver Alice
:language KIF
:ontology warehouse-stock
:content (= hdmi-ports (product television-50inch)) 3)
)

Explanation:

  • Bob informs Alice that the 50-inch televisions have 3 HDMI ports.

Learning Outcomes

This activity helped me understand how communication occurs between autonomous agents within agent-based systems. By constructing a dialogue between the agents Alice and Bob, I explored how the Knowledge Query and Manipulation Language (KQML) enables structured communication between agents through the use of performatives such as ask and tell. These performatives define the intention behind each message, allowing agents to request information or provide responses in a standardised format (Finin, Labrou and Mayfield, 1995).

In addition, the activity demonstrated how message content can be represented using the Knowledge Interchange Format (KIF). KIF provides a formal language for representing knowledge in a logical structure that can be interpreted and shared between intelligent systems (Genesereth and Fikes, 1992). In the dialogue created for this task, KIF expressions were used to represent knowledge about warehouse inventory and product specifications, such as the stock level of 50-inch televisions and the number of HDMI ports available.

Through this exercise, I developed a clearer understanding of how agent communication languages support interopability and knowledge exchange in distributed intelligent systems. Structured communication between agents is essential in multi-agent environments where different systems must collaborate and share information efficiently (Wooldridge, 2009). Overall, the activity illustrates how communication protocols and shared knowledge representation formats enable agents to coordinate actions and support automated decision-making processes.

Group Project

Group E Team Project: Multi-Agent Academic Research Online System – Design and Implementation Proposal

Overview

The team project was a formal group assessment requiring teams to design and propose an intelligent agent-based system for a defined domain. Group E was assigned the Academic Research Online domain. The deliverable was a design and implementation proposal for a multi-agent system, grounded in established agent theory and supported by UML diagrams, architectural decisions, and an identified set of challenges and limitations.

I served as a hybrid contributor, taking on both coordination responsibilities and technical authorship. My role included creating and circulating the initial structured outline of the proposal, maintaining meeting documentation across eight meetings, supporting document consistency during consolidation, and authoring the System Requirements and Conclusion sections across multiple iterations.


System Overview

How can a multi-agent system be designed to autonomously retrieve, evaluate, and structure academic research data from online sources, in a way that is modular, scalable, and transparent in its reasoning?


Architecture and Methodology

The proposed system adopts a Belief–Desire–Intention (BDI) architecture coordinated through a blackboard model, implemented in Python 3.9+. The system retrieves academic metadata from the ArXiv API, processes and evaluates retrieved records, and outputs structured JSON for downstream analysis and client reporting.

The architecture comprises four specialised agents:

  • SearchAgent — retrieves raw metadata from the ArXiv API.
  • ExtractionAgent — standardises raw XML responses into structured records.
  • ProcessingAgent — applies adaptive relevance filtering using a score-based evaluation of titles and summaries.
  • StorageAgent — persists processed results in JSON and plain-text formats.

Agents coordinate through a shared blackboard environment rather than direct communication, enabling loose coupling, observability of intermediate states, and flexible task progression. Each agent implements a perceive–deliberate–act cycle as an abstract base class, with intention reconsideration when quality thresholds are not met.


Key Design Decisions

  • The BDI model was selected over a monolithic pipeline for its explicit goal management, adaptability, and transparent reasoning (Wooldridge, 2009).
  • The blackboard architecture (Nii, 1986) was chosen over direct agent-to-agent communication to support modularity and observability.
  • Data sources were restricted to the publicly accessible ArXiv repository, in line with responsible data use principles (Floridi et al., 2018).
  • Rule-based quality heuristics were adopted at this stage as a deliberate trade-off, prioritising predictability and testability over learning-based sophistication, with ML extensions identified as future work.

My Individual Contribution

  • Created and circulated the initial structured outline of the design proposal (Meeting 2, 02/02/2026) to align the team and establish shared understanding of deliverables.
  • Maintained meeting documentation across Meetings 3–8, helping allocate sections to members’ strengths and supporting document consistency during revisions.

  • Authored the Conclusion section, framing the rule-based approach as part of a staged development strategy with clear pathways to learning-enhanced BDI agents.

Reflection

The team project was my first group assignment on the programme, and my teams had been randomly allocated, which initially produced genuine anxiety about whether collaboration would work effectively. In practice, Group E moved through the forming stage unusually smoothly: communication was open from the first meeting, responsibilities were distributed according to members’ strengths, and constructive feedback was exchanged throughout. Through Tuckman’s (1965) lens, the early investment in shared documentation and explicit role allocation helped the team reach a productive working stage quickly.

My hybrid role — spanning both coordination and technical authorship — meant that I experienced the proposal from two vantage points simultaneously. Coordinating the document across eight meetings reinforced how much the quality of a design proposal depends on team dynamics beyond its technical content: clear communication, collaborative review, and continuous alignment all materially improved what was submitted. Iterating the System Requirements section three times also taught me that specification documents in practice cannot be fixed early; they evolve alongside the design as understanding deepens (Sommerville, 2016).

One area I would approach differently in future group work is my behaviour during the forming stage. My initial anxiety made me more reserved than I would typically be, meaning I contributed less proactively in the early meetings until I became comfortable with the group. Recognising this pattern, I intend to treat the forming stage of future teams as an opportunity to contribute actively rather than a period to get through.

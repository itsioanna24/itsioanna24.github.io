Model Performance Measurement

1. Introduction

Evaluating machine learning models is essential to ensure reliability and robustness in real-world applications. Classification models are commonly assessed using the Receiver Operating Characteristic (ROC) curve and the Area Under the Curve (AUC), while regression models are evaluated using the coefficient of determination (R²).

The ROC curve evaluates the trade-off between true positive rate and false positive rate across thresholds, while AUC provides a threshold-independent measure of performance (Saito and Rehmsmeier, 2015). AUC is particularly useful when comparing models, although it may obscure performance differences in imbalanced datasets (Imani et al., 2026).

This report investigates how parameter changes affect AUC and R², while also addressing ethical and professional considerations in model evaluation.

2. AUC and ROC Analysis (Classification Performance)

2.1 Logistic Regression and Regularisation (C Parameter)

The parameter C controls the strength of regularisation in Logistic Regression, balancing model simplicity and data fitting (Géron, 2022).

Analysis

The results demonstrate that increasing C improves AUC. Lower values impose stronger regularisation, leading to underfitting and reduced performance. As C increases, the model becomes more flexible and better captures underlying patterns.
Higher AUC values indicate improved discrimination between classes, reflecting stronger predictive capability (Saito and Rehmsmeier, 2015).

Analysis

The linear kernel achieved the highest AUC, suggesting that the dataset is largely linearly separable. More complex kernels (RBF, polynomial) resulted in lower performance, likely due to overfitting or unnecessary model complexity.

This demonstrates that increased complexity does not always improve performance and may reduce generalisation (Bishop, 2006).

The proportion of training data significantly affects model performance and generalisation.

Analysis

Reducing the test size (increasing training data) improves AUC, as the model has more information to learn from. Conversely, larger test sizes reduce training data, leading to poorer performance.
This highlights the importance of adequate training data for reliable model evaluation (Goodfellow, Bengio and Courville, 2016).

3. R² Analysis (Regression Performance)

The R² metric measures the proportion of variance in the dependent variable explained by the model (James et al., 2021).

Analysis

R² is highly sensitive to prediction accuracy. When predictions closely match actual values, R² approaches 1, indicating strong explanatory power. Conversely, poor predictions result in lower or negative R² values.

In this experiment, inconsistencies in numerical outputs were observed due to variable reuse within the notebook. This highlights the importance of controlled experimental design and proper implementation.

4. Ethical, Legal and Professional Considerations

4.1 Reliability and Reproducibility

Reliable evaluation requires reproducible experiments. Inconsistent results, as observed in the R² section, demonstrate how poor experimental practices can lead to misleading conclusions.

4.2 Overfitting and Generalisation

The experiments showed that increasing model complexity does not always improve performance. Overfitting can lead to poor generalisation, which is critical in high-stakes applications such as healthcare and finance (Goodfellow, Bengio and Courville, 2016).

4.3 Limitations of AUC

Although widely used, AUC may not capture performance differences in imbalanced datasets, potentially masking poor model behaviour (Saito and Rehmsmeier, 2015; Imani et al., 2026).

4.4 Professional Responsibility

Machine learning professionals must ensure:

  • correct experimental design
  • proper validation
  • critical interpretation of results

Failure to do so may lead to biased, unreliable, or harmful systems.

5. Conclusion

This study demonstrated that:

  • Increasing C improves AUC but may introduce overfitting
  • Simpler models can outperform complex ones
  • Training data size significantly impacts performance
  • R² depends strongly on prediction accuracy and experimental design

Individual Presentation

Comparative Analysis of Deep Learning Approaches for Object Recognition Using CIFAR-10

Overview

This individual presentation examined how the choice of deep learning approach influences performance on an image classification task, using the CIFAR-10 dataset as a common benchmark. Three models were built, trained and evaluated under controlled conditions:

  • A Baseline CNN without regularisation or augmentation, used to establish a lower performance bound and illustrate the effect of overfitting.
  • An Improved CNN with increased filter depth, data augmentation, Batch Normalisation, Dropout, L2 regularisation and Global Average Pooling.
  • A Transfer Learning model based on MobileNetV2 pre-trained on ImageNet, fine-tuned in a two-phase training strategy.

The comparison was designed to answer the question: how much does the choice of deep learning approach affect performance, and what trade-offs each approach introduces between generalisation, training time, and data efficiency. The presentation was framed around Dubai's Roads and Transport Authority (RTA) smart traffic systems as a motivating real-world context for object recognition.

Across all three models, the cat and dog classes remained the most challenging, largely due to the limited 32×32 pixel resolution of CIFAR-10 where fine-grained features are lost. Frog, automobile and ship were consistently the strongest classes because their features remain visually distinguishable at low resolution.

Critical Reflection

The most significant learning from this presentation was recognising that the most complex model is not necessarily the best model for a given problem. The Improved CNN outperformed the MobileNetV2 transfer learning model by roughly three percentage points in both accuracy and F1 score, which initially surprised me given the literature's emphasis on pre-trained networks. On reflection, this result reflected an unfair comparison rather than a failure of transfer learning: MobileNetV2 was constrained to 64×64 inputs against its native 224×224 design, the transfer learning model had less training time and had not fully converged, and there is a domain gap between ImageNet and CIFAR-10. This taught me to interrogate my own results before accepting them and to articulate the conditions under which a model's performance can and cannot be trusted.

Preparing this presentation also developed my technical decision-making in a way that directly responded to the developmental feedback received on the Unit 6 group project. The tutor had noted that the discussion of algorithm selection could have been deeper; this time I explicitly justified each modelling choice in the narrative — the creation of a stratified 42,500 / 7,500 / 10,000 train/validation/test split to prevent data leakage, normalisation for gradient stability (Ioffe and Szegedy, 2015; Bjorck et al., 2018), the combination of L2 regularisation, Dropout (Srivastava et al., 2014) and Batch Normalisation to control the overfitting observed in the baseline, and the two-phase fine-tuning strategy for the transfer learning model (Goodfellow, Bengio and Courville, 2016). Structuring the argument this way was a direct application of the critical appraisal learning outcome on real-world ML problems under technical risk and uncertainty.

Framing the work around Dubai's RTA smart traffic systems also sharpened my professional awareness that deployment suitability is rarely reducible to accuracy alone. The Baseline CNN is unsuitable for deployment despite its simplicity because of overfitting; the Improved CNN performs well but requires large datasets and longer training; the Transfer Learning approach is the most deployment-ready because it is data-efficient and designed for edge devices.

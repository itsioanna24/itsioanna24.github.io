Clustering Activity

Reflection on K-means Clustering Visualisations

This activity enhanced my understanding of the iterative logic of the K-means clustering algorithm through two visualisations. The first animation (Shabalin, n.d.), clearly demonstrates the core steps of K-means: assigning data points to the nearest centroid and updating centroid positions based on cluster means until convergence. This reflects the algorithm’s objective of minimising within-cluster variance and grouping similar data points together (Ikotun et al., 2022).

The second visualisation (Harris, 2014), using the “I’ll choose” and “Uniform Points” options, highlighted the sensitivity of K-means to initial centroid selection. Different starting points produced different clustering outcomes, demonstrating that the algorithm may converge to local optima rather than a global solution (Pant, Leigh and Fajardo Rueda, 2025).

Comparing both animations emphasised that K-means performance depends heavily on dataset structure and parameter choices. This aligns with research showing that factors such as data distribution, feature relevance, and initialisation significantly affect clustering quality (Wannaporn Junthopas and Chantha Wongoutong, 2025). From a professional perspective, this highlights the importance of critically evaluating datasets and algorithm assumptions to ensure reliable and responsible machine learning applications.

Reflection on Jaccard Coefficient Calculations

This activity helped me understand how the Jaccard coefficient measures similarity between observations when working with binary and asymmetric data. In this exercise, categorical values were converted into binary form (Y/P = 1 and N/A = 0), while the symmetric attribute (Gender) was excluded from the calculation. The Jaccard coefficient focuses on shared presences of attributes and ignores joint absences, making it suitable for datasets where the presence of a feature carries more significance than its absence (Choi, Cha and Tappert, 2010).

The results showed that Jack and Mary had the highest similarity (0.667), followed by Jack and Jim (0.333), while Jim and Marry had the lowest similarity (0.25). This demonstrates how similarity measures help identify relationships between observations and support tasks such as clustering and pattern discovery in machine learning (Aggarwal, 2015).

This activity also highlights the importance of choosing appropriate similarity metrics and preparing datasets correctly, which is essential for machine learning professionals when analysing real-world data and ensuring responsible and reliable analytical outcomes.

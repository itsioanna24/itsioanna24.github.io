CNN Model Activity

1. Introduction

Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) are a fundamental deep learning approach widely used in image recognition tasks. Their ability to automatically extract hierarchical features from images has enabled advancements in domains such as autonomous systems, healthcare, and surveillance (LeCun, Bengio and Hinton, 2015). However, alongside their technical capabilities, CNNs raise important ethical, social, and professional concerns.

This ePortfolio critically reflects on the ethical implications of CNN technology based on Wall (2019) and evaluates the performance of a CNN model through practical experimentation using the CIFAR-10 dataset.

2. Ethical and Social Implications of CNNs

Wall (2019) highlights several ethical challenges associated with CNN-based systems, particularly in relation to bias, transparency, accountability, and privacy.

A major concern is bias in training data. CNNs learn patterns directly from datasets, and if these datasets are unbalanced or lack diversity, the model may produce biased outcomes. This can result in unequal performance across different categories or groups, raising issues of fairness and inclusivity (Goodfellow, Bengio and Courville, 2016).

Another significant issue is the lack of transparency and explainability. CNNs operate as “black-box” models, meaning their internal decision-making processes are not easily interpretable. As a result, it becomes difficult to justify predictions, raising concerns about accountability, particularly in high-stakes domains (Wall, 2019).

Additionally, CNN technologies can contribute to privacy concerns, especially in applications such as facial recognition and surveillance systems. The ability to automatically identify individuals from images can lead to misuse and reduced personal privacy.

Finally, there are broader societal implications, including over-reliance on automated systems and potential job displacement as AI systems increasingly replace human roles (LeCun, Bengio and Hinton, 2015).

3. Practical Experiment: CNN Object Recognition

To explore these issues in practice, a CNN model was implemented using the CIFAR-10 dataset (Krizhevsky, Sutskever and Hinton, 2012). The model was trained on labelled image data and evaluated using test images.

Different input images were selected by modifying the index of the test dataset, and the predicted labels were compared with the actual labels.

Interpretation of Results

The model correctly classified 4 out of 5 images (80% accuracy), indicating relatively strong performance on the selected samples.

Correct Predictions

The model successfully identified ship, frog, cat, and horse. This suggests that CNNs perform well when images contain clear and distinctive features that align with patterns learned during training (Goodfellow, Bengio and Courville, 2016).

Incorrect Prediction

The model incorrectly classified an airplane as a deer. This highlights limitations in the model’s ability to generalise to certain variations of objects.

Key Observations

1. Feature Misinterpretation

CNNs rely on visual patterns such as edges, textures, and shapes rather than true semantic understanding. This can lead to errors when different objects share similar visual characteristics (Goodfellow, Bengio and Courville, 2016).

2. Dataset Dependency

The misclassification suggests that the training dataset may not sufficiently represent all variations of certain classes, affecting model performance.

3. Generalisation Limitations

Although the model performs well on familiar patterns, it struggles with less typical inputs, which limits its reliability in real-world scenarios.

Model Variability and Reproducibility

Deep learning models such as CNNs are inherently non-deterministic, meaning that results may vary between different training runs. This is due to factors such as random weight initialisation, data shuffling, and optimisation processes (Goodfellow, Bengio and Courville, 2016).

As a result, the same input image may not always produce identical predictions across different executions of the model. This raises important considerations:

  • Reproducibility challenges, where results may differ between experiments
  • Reliability concerns, particularly in critical applications
  • The need for techniques such as controlled random seeds and repeated evaluations

5. Linking Practice to Ethical Implications

The experimental findings directly reflect the ethical concerns discussed earlier.

  • The incorrect prediction demonstrates how dataset limitations and bias can influence model outcomes (Wall, 2019)
  • The inability to explain predictions reinforces the issue of lack of transparency
  • The presence of errors highlights concerns about reliability and accountability in real-world applications

These observations confirm that ethical challenges in AI are closely linked to technical limitations.

6. Learning Outcomes Reflection

This activity enhanced understanding of both the technical and ethical aspects of machine learning.

It demonstrated the importance of addressing ethical issues such as bias, fairness, and transparency, while also highlighting the challenges associated with dataset quality and model generalisation. These insights are essential for machine learning professionals when developing and deploying AI systems responsibly.

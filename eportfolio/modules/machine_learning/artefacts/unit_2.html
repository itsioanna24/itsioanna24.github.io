Collaborative Discussion 1 - Peer Response 1

Discussion Forum

Reflection on this Artefact

Kapinga's response grounded my initial argument in established safety theory, introducing Perrow's (1999) concept of tight coupling and Hollnagel's (2017) work on reduced organisational buffers. This framing was particularly valuable because it moved the discussion from a single case-study observation into a broader theoretical register: the fragility I had identified in Toyota's Industry 4.0 architecture reflects a recognised pattern in highly optimised sociotechnical systems, not an isolated failure. Her emphasis on maintaining human expertise and hybrid human–machine collaboration also aligned with Amershi et al.'s (2019) argument that effective ML deployment depends on sustained human oversight and complementary design. Engaging with her point about implicit human knowledge being eroded by automation reinforced, for me, that Industry 5.0 is a strategic recalibration rather than an incremental technological change — a perspective I carried forward into my Unit 3 summary post.

Collaborative Discussion 1 - Peer Response 2

Discussion Forum

Reflection on this Artefact

Monique's response broadened the discussion by introducing cross-sector parallels, comparing Toyota's largely economic disruption with more serious incidents in healthcare and banking — including the 2021 Health Service Executive ransomware attack (US Department of Health and Human Services, 2022) and systemic IT failures in UK banking (UK Parliament Treasury Committee, 2025). These examples illustrated that the consequences of tightly coupled digital infrastructures extend well beyond financial performance metrics and can translate into direct societal and human harm. Her emphasis on governance reform, incident reporting requirements, and regulatory alignment challenged me to consider resilience as an institutional property, not only an architectural one. This strengthened my sense that Industry 5.0's emphasis on resilience, human oversight and ethical governance is a necessity rather than an aspiration, and reinforced the professional responsibility dimension.

Collaborative Discussion 1 - Peer Response 3

Discussion Forum

Reflection on this Artefact

Naeema's response reframed the discussion at the model level, introducing a dimension that neither my original post nor the earlier peer replies had fully explored. Her argument was that while models may perform well under stable conditions, their dependence on continuous, high-quality data streams makes them inherently vulnerable to disruption — and that the real challenge is ensuring model outputs degrade safely and predictably, rather than only recovering the surrounding system (Amodei et al., 2016). This pushed my thinking from architectural and organisational resilience toward algorithmic resilience. Her extension of the argument — that ML performance should be evaluated through robustness under uncertainty, transparency during failure, and support for human decision-making, rather than accuracy alone — directly aligns with human–AI interaction research (Amershi et al., 2019).

My Responses to Peers

Collaborative Discussion 1 - Peer Response To Sarka

Discussion Forum

Reflection on this Artefact

Engaging with Sarka's post on the 2017 WannaCry attack on the NHS shifted my thinking from the operational consequences of ML disruption toward its clinical and ethical consequences. Her framing of the incident as a patient-safety issue rather than a pure IT failure helped me see that ML systems embedded in diagnostics or resource allocation can fail silently when data pipelines degrade, producing harm that is less visible than an outright system outage (Finlayson et al., 2021). Writing this response deepened my understanding that resilience in ML must be engineered across both the infrastructure layer and the algorithmic layer, and that systems deployed in high-stakes domains should be designed for safe degradation rather than catastrophic failure (Amodei et al., 2016; Wiens et al., 2019).

Collaborative Discussion 1 - Peer Response To Andreea

Discussion Forum

Reflection on this Artefact

Responding to Andreea's post on the NotPetya cyberattack extended my thinking about how machine learning models should be evaluated in industrial settings. Her example made it clear that supply-chain disruption is not only a logistics concern but also a data concern, since optimisation-driven ML pipelines depend on uninterrupted, high-quality data flows. Engaging with this idea pushed me to consider how standard performance metrics, such as accuracy or recall, may conceal fragility when pipelines are disrupted, aligning with the concerns raised by Amodei et al. (2016) on AI safety and Goodfellow, Shlens and Szegedy (2014) on adversarial robustness. Writing this response strengthened my professional awareness that resilience is not an external safeguard but a performance characteristic in its own right.

Group Project

Group D Team Project: Optimising Airbnb Pricing Strategies in New York City

Overview

Unit 6 was the first formal team assessment of the module. The brief required groups to propose an insightful business question using the Airbnb NYC dataset and address it through Exploratory Data Analysis, data preprocessing, statistical analysis, visualisation and clustering, producing a concise analytical report aimed at Airbnb executives.

Group D implemented Track 1 (Regression & Clustering). I served as Project Lead, with responsibilities including drafting the team contract, coordinating six virtual meetings across different time zones, maintaining meeting minutes and shared tracking documents, supporting the integration of individual analytical components, and producing the final consolidated report.


Business Question

How can Airbnb optimise pricing strategies across New York City based on location, property characteristics and availability patterns, and what actionable listing segments can be identified using clustering techniques?


Methodology

The analysis used the AB_NYC_2019 dataset from Kaggle, containing 48,884 cleaned Airbnb listings across New York City. Preprocessing included removing zero-price errors, imputing missing review values, converting last_review into a numerical days_since_review feature, and capping minimum_nights at 365. Given the heavily right-skewed price distribution (median $106, mean $153), the price variable was log-transformed before modelling.

Two classical machine learning approaches were applied:

  • Multivariate linear regression — to model price prediction using interpretable features including borough, neighbourhood, latitude, longitude, room type, minimum nights, number of reviews, review frequency, host portfolio size and availability indicators.
  • K-Means clustering (with DBSCAN comparison) — to identify natural market segments of listings, with the optimal number of clusters (k = 4) selected using the Elbow Method.

Key Findings

  • The multivariate linear regression model achieved R² ≈ 0.53 and RMSE ≈ 0.475 on the log scale, indicating moderate predictive performance and confirming that Airbnb pricing follows structured patterns while still being influenced by local variation.
  • Borough and room type were the strongest predictors of price, with Manhattan entire-home listings consistently occupying the premium tier.
  • K-Means clustering identified four practical market segments: Low Activity Listings, Standard Listings, Investment Listings and High Demand Value Listings.
  • DBSCAN produced a low silhouette score (≈ 0.20), suggesting that the Airbnb market behaves more as a continuous economic spectrum than as sharply separated natural clusters — making K-Means the more useful segmentation approach for management insights.

Business Recommendations

The analysis supported a recommendation for Airbnb to adopt a segment-aware, location-sensitive pricing strategy rather than a single city-wide rule. High-demand listings should focus on occupancy optimisation, investment listings on yield and stay-length optimisation, and low-activity listings on improved listing quality and promotional pricing.

Reflection

Leading this team project developed my understanding of applying machine learning to a real-world dataset within a distributed team. The Airbnb NYC dataset, with its large size, skewed price distribution and mixed features, required careful preprocessing decisions, including removing erroneous zero-price entries, handling missing values, engineering time-based features, and capping unrealistic values. These choices were not mechanical but needed to be justified in relation to the business problem, and discussing them as a team strengthened my ability to defend analytical decisions.

This experience highlighted challenges related to dataset applicability. The skewed price distribution required log transformation, the concentration of listings in specific boroughs limited generalisability, and ambiguous variables such as availability_365 introduced uncertainty that could not be resolved from the data alone. This reinforced that model performance depends not only on algorithm choice but on how well its assumptions align with the dataset. The low DBSCAN silhouette score further illustrated that the data did not naturally form discrete clusters.

Working in a distributed team required structured coordination, including clear roles, scheduled meetings, shared documentation and recorded sessions. This experience strengthened my ability to collaborate effectively in a virtual environment and to balance independent judgement with collective decision-making.

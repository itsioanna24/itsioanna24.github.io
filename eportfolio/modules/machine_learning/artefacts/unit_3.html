Correlation and Regression Activity

Covariance Pearson Correlation

About dataset:

  • data1 -> Normally distributed around 100, std ≈ 20
  • data2 -> equals data1 PLUS:
    • random noise (std ≈ 10)
    • constant shift of +50

Results:

data1: mean=100.776 stdv=19.620

data2: mean=151.050 stdv=22.358

Covariance: 389.755

Pearsons correlation: 0.888

Output explained:

Pearson r = 0.888 -> This means:

  • Strong positive linear correlation
  • But not perfect (because of noise)

Covariance = 389.755 -> This means:

  • Covariance is positive -> variables increase together

Experiment 1 — Reduce Noise

I changed:

data2 = data1 + (2 * randn(1000) + 50)

(IMAGE)

Instead of noise with std = 10, now noise std=2.

Results:

data1: mean=100.776 stdv=19.620

data2: mean=150.831 stdv=19.773

Covariance: 386.217

Pearsons correlation: 0.995

Output explained:

By reducing noise:

  • Random variation reduced
  • The linear relationship became clearer
  • Scatter points tightened around a straight line

Covariance didn’t change dramatically because:

  • The main relationship (data2 ~= data1 + 50) is still there
  • Scale didn’t change much

But Pearson correlation increased because: Correlation standardizes covariance by dividing by standard deviations.

Less noise -> smaller random variance -> stronger standardized relationship.

Reflection

Reducing the stochastic noise in the second variable increased Pearson’s correlation from 0.888 to 0.995, demonstrating that correlation strength is highly sensitive to variance introduced by measurement noise. The scatter plot showed near-perfect linear alignment, confirming that Pearson’s coefficient approaches 1 as random disturbance decreases. This illustrates the importance of data quality in machine learning systems, as noise directly affects the reliability of inferred relationships.

The scatter plot showed substantial dispersion, illustrating how poor data quality may reduce the predictive power of linear models.

    • Data entry errors

    • Robust modelling

Linear Regression

Dataset and result:

x = [5,7,8,7,2,17,2,9,4,11,12,9,6]

y = [99,86,87,88,111,86,103,87,94,78,77,85,86]

Result:

Results:

Pearsons correlation: -0.759

  • Strong negative correlation
  • As x increases -> y decreases
  • Regression line slopes downward

-0.759:

  • Strong inverse linear relationship
  • About 76% strength
  • Not perfect but meaningful

R2 = r2 = (-0.759)2 ~ 0.576 ~ 57.6% of variance in y is explained by x

Experiment 1 — Add an outlier

I changed:

x.append(30)

y.append(200)

Results:

  • Pearson r = +0.666
  • Relationship flipped to positive
  • Predicted value at x = 10 ~ 99.89
  • Regression line now slopes upward

One single data point changed:

  • Direction of relationship
  • Correlation strength
  • Prediction
  • Interpretation

OLS (Ordinary Least Squares) minimises squared errors

Because the outlier is:

  • Far right (large x)
  • Very high y

High-leverage points dominate slope estimation.

Reflection

Introducing a single high-leverage outlier (30, 200) reversed the sign of the correlation coefficient from -0.759 to +0.666 and significantly altered the regression slope. This demonstrates the extreme sensitivity of Ordinary Least Squares regression to influential observations. The predicted value at x=10 shifted from 85.59 to 99.89, highlighting how outliers can distort model interpretation and predictive reliability.

Learning Outcomes

This experiment directly demonstrates:

  1. Dataset Challenges
    • Small datasets are unstable
    • Outliers distort model structure
    • Linear regression assumes homoscedasticity & no extreme influence
  2. Legal / Ethical / Professional Issues

    In real-world ML systems, failure to detect influential outliers may result in biased decision-making. In contexts such as financial risk assessment, hiring systems, or healthcare predictions, a small number of anomalous data points could distort model outcomes, leading to unfair or harmful consequences. Machine learning professionals have a responsibility to conduct diagnostic analysis and ensure data robustness before deployment.

Experiment 2 — Remove the Natural High-Leverage Point (x = 17)

x.remove(17)

y.remove(86)

Results:

  • Pearson r = -0.571
  • Prediction at x = 10 -> 84.46
  • Negative slope but weaker

The correlation became weaker -0.571 instead of -0.759.

Reflection

Removing a high-leverage point that aligned with the overall negative trend reduced the magnitude of the correlation from -0.759 to -0.571, indicating that not all influential observations are harmful. In contrast, introducing a single inconsistent extreme outlier reversed the correlation sign and significantly altered predictions. This demonstrates the distinction between leverage and influence, highlighting the necessity of diagnostic analysis in regression modelling.

Learning Outcomes

This experiment directly demonstrates:

  1. Dataset Applicability
    • Small datasets are structurally fragile
    • Influential points can stabilise or destabilise models
    • Model diagnostics (Cook’s distance, leverage statistics) are essential
  2. Ethical & Professional Responsibility
    • Removing data blindly may distort findings
    • Keeping anomalous but valid data is sometimes correct
    • Model auditing is part of professional ML practice

Experiment 1 — Remove one predictor

This experiment investigates how removing one predictor variable (Volume) from a multiple linear regression model affects coefficient estimates and predictions. The aim is to examine model sensitivity and how regression coefficients depend on model specification.

I changed:

X = df[['Weight']]

And predict:

regr.predict([[2300]])

Results:

  • New Weight coefficient: 0.01699973
  • Intercept: 80.0594
  • • Prediction for (Weight = 2300kg): 119.16 g CO₂
Model Weight Coefficient Prediction (2300kg)
Weight + Volume 0.00755 107.21
Weight Only 0.01699 119.16

Several important changes occurred after removing Volume:

  • The Weight coefficient more than doubled (from 0.00755 to 0.01699).
  • The predicted CO₂ emission increased by approximately 12 grams.
  • The estimated impact of vehicle weight changed substantially.

In the original model, the Weight coefficient represented the effect of Weight while controlling for Volume. After removing Volume, the coefficient reflects the total association between Weight and CO₂ emissions without accounting for other explanatory factors.

This demonstrates that regression coefficients are conditional on the variables included in the model. Changing the set of predictors alters both coefficient magnitude and predictions.

Learning Outcomes

  1. Dataset Challenges
    • Regression coefficients are sensitive to model specification.
    • Feature selection influences interpretation.
    • Removing predictors can significantly alter estimated relationships.
    • Model conclusions depend on which variables are included.
  2. Legal, Ethical & Professional Issues
    • Misinterpreting regression coefficients may lead to incorrect conclusions about variable importance.
    • Removing relevant predictors without justification may distort results.
    • Decisions based on incomplete models could lead to flawed regulatory, financial, or environmental policies.

Experiment 2 — Diagnosing Multicollinearity

print(df[['Weight','Volume']].corr())

Results:

Weight Volume
Weight 1.000 0.7535
Volume 0.7535 1.000

So: Weight–Volume correlation = 0.7535

This indicates a strong positive association between predictors. Such correlation confirms the presence of multicollinearity in the model. Because Weight and Volume are strongly related, their explanatory effects on CO₂ emissions overlap. This explains why removing Volume caused the Weight coefficient to increase substantially. When correlated predictors are included simultaneously, their individual contributions become conditional and sensitive to model specification.

Learning Outcomes

  1. Dataset Challenges
    • Correlated predictors undermine coefficient interpretability.
    • Regression coefficients are conditional, not absolute.
    • Diagnostic testing (correlation matrices) is necessary before interpretation.
  2. Legal, Ethical & Professional Issues
    • Misinterpreting inflated coefficients may distort perceived variable importance.
    • Responsible ML practice requires reporting predictor correlations and justifying model structure.

Experiment 3 — Overfitting (Degree 10)

I changed:

mymodel = numpy.poly1d(numpy.polyfit(x, y, 10))

Results:

Compare across experiments:

R² increased from 0.957 → 0.992.

Reflection

A tenth-degree polynomial model was fitted to the dataset. The resulting coefficient of determination was:

R²=0.9919

Although this value is very close to 1, the plotted curve shows noticeable oscillations and increased sensitivity to small fluctuations in the data.

The model fits the training data extremely closely but at the cost of increased complexity and reduced stability. With only 18 data points and 11 parameters, the model exhibits high variance and begins to capture noise rather than the underlying trend.

This demonstrates overfitting: a model that performs exceptionally well on observed data but may generalise poorly to new observations.

Learning Outcomes

  1. Dataset Challenges
    • Small datasets are vulnerable to overfitting..
    • High R² does not guarantee good generalisation.
    • Model complexity must be balanced with sample size.
  2. Legal, Ethical & Professional Issues

    Overfitted models can appear highly accurate during development yet perform poorly in real-world deployment. In safety-critical or regulatory domains, such instability may lead to unreliable or misleading predictions.

    Machine learning professionals must therefore:

    • alidate models using separate test data,
    • Avoid relying solely on training performance metrics,
    • Control model complexity to ensure generalisability.

Reflection Across Experiments

  1. Introduction
    Across the four practical notebooks (correlation, simple regression, multiple regression, and polynomial regression), I explored how data characteristics and model specification influence statistical relationships, coefficient interpretation, predictive behaviour, and model reliability.

    • Sensitivity to noise

    • Model complexity and overfitting
